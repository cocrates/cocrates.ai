# Chapter 004: Symbiosis

## Episode 001: Collision

The deafening roar of the helicopter blades tore through the air.

James Mercer leaped out of the cabin before the aircraft had completely settled on the makeshift pad. The wind whipped violently against his face—carrying dust, scattered debris, and the sharp, acrid stench of burning materials. High-pitched screams and wailing sirens echoed through the dark Manhattan streets. First responders barked urgent updates into their radios, their voices overlapping in a frantic, chaotic symphony.

"Dr. Mercer!" the on-scene commander shouted, jogging up to him. His bright red helmet was streaked with soot, and sweat glistened on his charred face. "A five-story building collapsed in a chain reaction. We have massive structural entrapments. The triage center is set up right over—"

"What about the casualties?" James interrupted, his clinical tone cutting through the noise.

"Twelve pulled out so far. Three critical patients have already been transported. We estimate at least five or six more are still buried deep beneath the rubble."

James nodded curtly, turning his steps toward the triage tent. But he froze after a few paces.

Up ahead, perched atop a massive pile of fractured concrete, was a woman.

She wore a high-visibility yellow vest over her work clothes, paired with a heavy utility belt, and held a radio tight against her ear. She was not a member of the NYPD rescue unit or the fire department—the logo stamped on her vest read *NY Medical Center*. Her eyes were locked onto the deep, dark crevices of the debris, searching. In her hand, a portable diagnostic transceiver blinked with a faint, steady green light.

James paused, narrowing his eyes. *Who is she?*

Her voice carried over the din, addressing an NYPD rescue officer nearby. Her tone was remarkably clear, calm, and deliberate.

"An R-17 unit is trapped right below this slab. It was actively operating on-site right up until the collapse. Its local memory contains the exact structural layout of the building and—"

*R-17.*

James’s expression hardened. He marched up the slope toward her.

"Are you seriously prioritizing a machine right now?"

The woman turned her head. She was young, with pale skin and dark circles of exhaustion framing her eyes. Her sharp gaze swept over James. She didn't look startled by his intrusion; instead, she seemed to be assessing him, coolly evaluating his presence.

"Without this android's data, it will take the rescue teams at least three additional hours to locate the survivors," she replied, her voice remaining perfectly level. "Do you want to guess how many of them will die in those three hours?"

James’s remaining patience evaporated. He had barely finished a grueling overnight cardiothoracic surgery at Chelsea's NY Medical Center when the emergency sirens summoned him here at dawn. Wearing only a light jacket thrown over his blood-flecked scrubs, he smelled of antiseptics and dried sweat. He held nothing but a single trauma kit. And this woman was talking about an android? Here? Now?

"Who are you?" James asked, his voice sharp with irritation. "What are you even doing here?"

She calmly pulled a security badge from her vest pocket and held it out. *NY Medical Center — Robotics & AI Integration Lab. Nora Sinclair.*

They worked for the same hospital.

James stared at the badge, briefly speechless. Yet his frustration didn't subside; it flared hotter. A colleague from his own institution, standing in the middle of a literal graveyard-in-progress, fussing over synthetic steel.

"Nora," James said, his voice dropping to a low, stern register. "People are dying. There are human beings suffocating beneath these blocks as we speak. And your concern is an android?"

Nora held his gaze. Her eyes did not waver in the slightest.

"Dr. Mercer, the R-17 is a specialized rescue android. It was collecting telemetry and structural metrics up until the millisecond the ceiling buckled. Building schematics, survivor coordinates, thermal signatures—everything is preserved in its local memory module. If we don't retrieve that data—"

"Data?" James dropped his trauma kit onto a flat piece of stone. "You're talking about data? The rescue team is digging with their hands. They need a doctor to keep people alive, and you're chasing code?"

"Without that code, they are digging blindly. Finding a single survivor will take—"

"So you want us to wait while you dig up a broken robot?"

Nora’s lips pressed into a tight, thin line. She stared at James for a long moment. Then, with deliberate, chilling slowness, she spoke.

"The R-17's memory module logged the exact shifting of the weight before the collapse. It knows where the air pockets are. It knows who was breathing where. If we guess wrong, the next slab we lift could crush the remaining survivors. Now, are you going to let me do my job?"

James snatched his trauma kit back up.

"Fine, Nora. Go save your machine. I'm going to save the people."

He turned on his heel.

"Humans first."

Leaving her with those words, James strode down toward the triage tent. He heard her faint voice call out behind him, but he refused to look back.

Yet as he walked away, a second voice drifted through the cold air—quieter, perfectly measured, and devoid of human breath, yet oddly resonant.

"Nora, the R-17 signal is locked. Three meters directly below your position."

James paused for a fraction of a second. He fought the sudden urge to look back, steeled his shoulders, and resumed his stride.

Behind him, Nora's voice rang out, brief and commanding.

"Iris, prepare the recovery kit."

The metallic clatter of tools being laid out on the concrete echoed through the ruins. Then, Nora's quiet murmur, almost to herself:

"The memory module is intact... barely."

James stepped inside the heavy canvas of the triage tent. The howling sirens outside faded into a muffled hum, replaced instantly by the agonizing groans of the wounded.

He washed his hands. He snapped on his surgical gloves.

*That woman. The one obsessed with machines. Her eyes. Unflinching.*

James shook his head vigorously. He had to focus. Right now, only the patients mattered.

## Episode 002: Paths (각자)

On top of the unstable mound of debris, Nora knelt in the gray dust.

This was the exact spot where the R-17 had been buried. RE-07 Iris’s hand-held diagnostic array hummed, emitting a rhythmic, high-pitched ping—*beep, beep, beep*—at precise intervals.

"Here. Two meters down," Iris said, pointing her synthetic finger toward a narrow gap between two crushed concrete slabs. Her voice was flat and unchanging—precise, calm, and utterly devoid of unnecessary inflection.

Nora reached her hand deep into the dark crevice. A jagged piece of rebar scraped against her wrist, tearing her skin. She winced but didn't pull back. Her fingertips brushed past cracked drywall, pulverized insulation, and then... a faint, mechanical heat.

The R-17's core memory module. It was still powered.

"Iris, sync the module’s lock coordinates."

Iris instantly transmitted the spatial data to Nora's optical interface. Nora shifted her hand, her fingers finding the outer edge of the rectangular module. She felt the safety latch. The manual release button.

*Click.*

"Got it."

Nora carefully withdrew her hand, holding a metallic brick the size of a paperback book. It was warm to the touch—the final, desperate logs of the R-17 were still radiating heat.

She stared at the module. Within this block lay the final seconds before the world fell apart. What the R-17 saw—the panic, the shifting pillars, the thermal blooms of human bodies caught in the dark.

"Iris, back to the mobile tech van. Now."

They scrambled down the rubble heap and hurried into the back of the parked hospital van. Inside, harsh fluorescent bulbs flickered to life, bathing the workspace in cold white light. Nora slotted the memory module into the diagnostic dock. Iris routed the heavy interface cables with practiced, fluid efficiency. The main monitor flared, and a massive waterfall of system logs began to cascade down the screen.

"Building structural blueprint—secured," Nora murmured, her fingers flying across the physical keyboard—a rapid, rhythmic *clack-clack-clack-clack* that matched her racing pulse. "Thermal telemetry—recovered. Life-sign sensor logs—syncing."

As the progress bar crept toward one hundred percent, Nora spoke in a hushed tone, her eyes glued to the screen.

"According to the Robot Charter, an android must prioritize human life above all else. Retrieving this data... it’s not just tech support, Iris. It's the highest execution of the Charter's mandate."

She struck the enter key with a sharp snap. The wireframe model of the collapsed building bloomed on the display, overlaid with glowing thermal heatmaps. Eight distinct crimson-and-gold clusters pulsed in the darkness—human bodies, still radiating life.

"Right here..." Nora’s voice caught in her throat. "Eight live signals. Locked in."

Iris leaned in, her artificial eyes scanning the coordinates. "I am routing this to the NYPD Rescue command."

Nora nodded, her hands already converting the raw coordinates into practical rescue pathways—depth estimates, structural hazards, and optimal entry angles.

While the transfer processed, she cast a brief glance through the van’s reinforced window. Across the muddy lot, the triage tent stood illuminated. Through the translucent canvas, she could see James Mercer moving like a blur—sorting patients, barking orders to nurses, his hands coated in dark blood.

She watched him for a quiet moment.

*The arrogant doctor. Let's see if his hands can keep up with my data.*

She pulled her gaze away, returning her focus to the terminal. A few minutes later, the data transmission was finalized. Nora stood up, grabbing her tablet.

"I'm delivering this to the site commander personally."

She stepped out of the van into the chilly, dust-laden air. The rescue radios were already buzzing with new life.

"Sector 3, structural coordinates received. We have the air pockets. Commencing dig."

Nora clutched the tablet tightly against her chest, walking toward the command post. Her mind drifted back to the doctor. James Mercer. She knew that name. A brilliant thoracic surgeon, but stubbornly anchored to the old ways.

She walked faster.

---

Inside the triage tent, James worked without a single pause.

"Apply pressure there. Start an IV line—now!"

The wounded kept coming. Most were minor—cuts, simple fractures, shock. But the critical ones demanded absolute focus. A middle-aged man with a crushed pelvis and suspected internal bleeding; an elderly woman drifting in and out of consciousness.

James’s hands never shook. He moved with clinical precision, his voice a steady anchor for the panicked residents and nurses around him. He didn't ask for a break, and he didn't look at the clock.

His chest pocket remained empty—no smart devices, no HUD glasses. Just raw human instinct and decades of training.

---

Back at the tech van, Nora finished the secondary data routing. The rescue channels were alight with activity.

"Sector 3, target located. Matches the R-17 telemetry perfectly. Extraction in progress."

"Sector 5, we have two pulses. Confirmed via R-17 thermal logs."

Nora rested her hands on the edge of the console and closed her eyes. It worked.

She opened them to find Iris standing at the van door, looking out toward the active excavation site.

"Nora."

"Yeah?"

"...Nothing. I am glad the data survived."

Nora nodded, picking up her tablet. "I should hand over the medical-specific telemetry to the lead surgeon."

The surgeon. James Mercer. She hesitated for a beat, then stepped down into the dust.

## Episode 003: Data (데이터)

Outside the triage tent, James finished securing a temporary splint. He pulled off his soiled gloves and stepped into the cool dawn air.

The dust was finally settling, leaving a gray film over the street. The sirens still wailed, but the frantic edge of the disaster had begun to dull into a systematic recovery operation.

The NYPD rescue band crackled over the nearby command radio.

"Sector 3, telemetry confirmed. Patient extracted. Alive."

"Sector 5, both pulses recovered. Moving to triage. Credit the R-17 telemetry."

James’s hands froze as he went to wash them at the portable station.

He said nothing, but his ears strained to catch the rest of the radio chatter.

An NYPD lieutenant walked up to him, wiping sweat from his forehead with a dirty sleeve.

"Dr. Mercer, we have the extracted survivors coming your way. Here are their preliminary medical profiles."

The lieutenant handed him a printed sheet—a localized data report detailing the trapped patients' vitals and estimated crush times. James scanned the paper.

The layout, the depth estimates, the precise physiological stress markers—it was a perfect match for the sketches Nora had tried to show him earlier.

"Who compiled this?" James asked.

"The hospital's tech team," the lieutenant replied. "Pulled it straight out of the trapped android’s memory core. Saved us at least three hours of digging. If we’d gone in blind, we’d still be clearing the upper joists."

Three hours.

James stared at the paper. Three hours of oxygen, of slowly bleeding out, saved by a sequence of ones and zeros.

His mind flashed back to his harsh words to Nora: *"Are you seriously prioritizing a machine right now?"*

He folded the paper quietly and tucked it into his scrub pocket. He turned to head back into the tent, but another voice came through the lieutenant’s radio.

"All eight locate points cleared. All survivors accounted for. If we didn't have that data..." The radio sputtered with static before the officer concluded, "...tell the tech team they saved some lives today."

James stood still, listening to the static.

He looked across the chaotic yard toward the ruins. Nora and Iris were still there, silhouetted against the rising sun. Nora was logging diagnostics on her tablet, while Iris systematically packing away the heavy recovery gear.

He watched them for a long, silent moment.

Then, he stepped back into the shadows of the triage tent.

Against his chest, the folded paper crinkled slightly with every breath he took.

## Episode 004: Guidance (가이드)

The flap of the triage tent flew open, and a stretcher was rushed inside.

"Critical patient!" a paramedic yelled. "Forty-year-old male, pulled from Sector 3. Crushed under a concrete joist. Unresponsive. Pulse is weak and thready. Abdomen is rigid and severely distended—we have massive internal bleeding."

James checked the vitals. The patient’s blood pressure was crashing. There was no time to wait for an ambulance to fight through the gridlocked Manhattan traffic to NY Medical Center.

"Prepare for immediate laparotomy. We're doing it here," James commanded.

The nurse gasped. "Doctor, this is a field tent. We don't have a sterile field, the lighting is inadequate, and we don't have enough blood units—"

"He won't survive the ride to Chelsea. Prep him now."

James snapped on a fresh pair of gloves. There was no CT scanner, no portable X-ray, no surgical theater. Just a single overhead light, a basic tray of instruments, and his own instincts.

For a brief second, his hands tensed. He closed his eyes, took a deep breath, and released the tension.

In a standard operating room, he would have Julian—the hospital's advanced SR-03 surgical assistant android—projecting real-time anatomical overlays directly onto the patient's skin. Here, in the wild, he had nothing but his own eyes and his fingers to navigate the bloody maze of a ruptured abdomen.

But he had to do it.

James took the scalpel.

"Incision."

He cut. As the abdominal cavity opened, dark blood pooled rapidly, obscuring his field of view. He needed to find the source of the hemorrhage, but without a high-volume suction unit, he was working blind. Sweat beaded on his forehead.

"More light. Clamp—now!"

His fingers probed the warm fluid, searching for the ruptured vessel. The patient's vitals monitor beeped frantically. *BP 90 over 50... 80 over 40.* The heart was failing. If he didn't find the leak in the next sixty seconds, the patient would code.

"Dr. Mercer."

A voice call broke through his panic from the tent entrance.

James looked up. RE-07 Iris stood there.

"I am not in the mood for—"

"Julian requested my intervention," Iris said smoothly. "He routed his surgical assistance protocols to me. I am here to assist."

James stared at her. Iris’s synthetic eyes were calm, glass-like, and absolutely steady. She was already holding up a pair of sterile gloves.

"Do you have any surgical assist logs?" James asked, his voice tight.

"I am currently synced with Julian's active database," Iris replied.

A single second of hesitation. James nodded.

"Get in here. Sterile gloves. Follow my hands—do exactly as I command."

Iris washed her hands with antiseptics and donned the gloves with flawless, mechanical fluidness. She stepped up to the opposite side of the steel table.

"Forceps."

Before James could even finish the syllable, the instrument was placed perfectly into his palm. It was as if she had anticipated the exact movement of his muscles before he even knew he was going to make them.

James glanced at her, but there was no time to marvel. He plunged his hands back into the blood.

Suddenly, the small LED indicator behind Iris's ear pulsed with a soft, brilliant blue light. For a fraction of a millisecond, her entire posture froze—a brief, quase-imperceptible pause as she downloaded Julian's real-time calculations and synced her internal motor pathways with James's surgical style.

"The laceration is on the posterior wall of the splenic artery," Iris said, her voice a cool, flat contrast to the chaos of the tent. "Two millimeters below your left index finger."

James shifted his hand, guiding his clamps to the exact spot she described.

He clamped down.

The geyser of blood instantly stopped.

James didn't say a word. He worked rapidly to ligate the artery, while Iris’s hands moved in perfect harmony with his, passing sutures, cutting thread, and clearing the field with the efficiency of a machine designed solely for this purpose.

By the time they placed the final suture, the patient's blood pressure had stabilized. The heart monitor settled into a steady, comforting rhythm.

James pulled off his gloves, his hands trembling slightly from the sheer adrenaline.

He looked at Iris.

"...Thank you."

Iris offered a polite, shallow bow. She did not say a word.

James stared into her synthetic eyes—the smooth, flawless irises. For a fleeting second, he thought he saw a flicker of something human behind them. But he didn't ask.

He simply nodded.

---

At the same time, on the opposite side of the collapse site.

Nora was working alongside an NYPD rescue crew near a partially collapsed cellar stairwell. The concrete overhead groaned ominously. The crew captain yelled, "Get back! The joists are shifting!"

Nora ignored him. She reached into a narrow crawlspace, pulling away jagged pieces of shattered masonry. A sharp edge of slate sliced across her forearm, drawing a bright red line of blood. She didn't stop. She kept clearing the path.

A dust-covered hand reached out from the dark hole.

"I have someone! Over here!" Nora screamed.

She grabbed the hand, hauling a young girl out of the suffocating space. As she pulled her clear, a shower of concrete fragments rained down, striking Nora's arm and deepening the gash. Blood began to soak through her sleeve.

But the child was safe.

Nora clutched her bleeding arm and looked up at the rescue team.

"Take her to the triage tent. Now."

---

James stepped out of the triage tent. The surgery had been a success; the patient was stable and awaiting transport. He breathed in the cool dawn air, letting it clear the smell of blood from his lungs.

His eyes scanned the lot and landed on Nora.

She was sitting on a plastic crate near the mobile tech van. Her forearm was gashed and bleeding, and Iris was carefully setting out medical supplies—antiseptic wipes, gauze, and bandages.

Nora looked up, sensing his presence.

Their eyes met across the dusty expanse.

James stared at her bloody arm. He didn't know how she had gotten hurt, but he saw the raw pain in her expression, and the way she held his gaze without flinching.

For two long seconds, neither of them spoke.

Then, James looked away.

Without a word, he turned and went back into the triage tent.

Nora watched his retreating figure, her expression hardening.

Iris gently took Nora's hand, pressing an alcohol wipe against the wound. Nora winced slightly as the chemical stung her flesh.

"Let me know if this hurts," Iris said softly.

"I'm fine."

Iris began wrapping the clean white gauze around Nora's arm with slow, methodical care. She didn't say anything else.

But Nora kept her eyes fixed on the entrance of the triage tent, where the doctor had disappeared.

## Episode 005: Fracture (균열)

The sky was turning a pale, cold blue as dawn broke over Manhattan.

The rescue operation was drawing to a close. All eight survivors had been pulled from the wreckage. The critical cases had been transported to Chelsea, and the minor injuries were fully processed.

Nora stood near the temporary helipad, waiting for the transport back to the hospital. A pristine white bandage was wrapped around her arm—Iris's flawless work. Iris stood a few feet away, double-checking the locks on the equipment crates.

Heavy footsteps sounded on the gravel.

James Mercer was walking toward the helipad. He, too, was heading back to NY Medical Center. As he approached, their paths crossed once more.

Nora was the first to speak.

"Good work out there, Dr. Mercer."

James stopped, looking at her.

"You too, Nora."

The exchange was brief, stiff, and laden with unspoken tension.

After a moment of awkward silence, James added:

"The R-17 telemetry... it made a difference."

Nora offered a small, quiet nod.

"I'm glad."

There was nothing left to say.

James climbed into the waiting helicopter. He buckled himself into the seat and pulled out his tablet to check the hospital’s internal network. Immediately, a stream of urgent, whispered articles about a high-profile sexual harassment scandal involving the Chief of Medicine, Dr. Steven Hart, flooded his feed. James scowled, his thumb quickly swiping the notifications away.

"Not my problem. I have enough to worry about," he muttered to himself, shoving the tablet back into his coat pocket. He was exhausted. He had zero interest in office politics or administrative scandals.

As the helicopter’s rotors began to spin faster, James looked down through the window. Nora was standing on the asphalt below, her back turned to him. The white bandage on her arm caught the first rays of the morning sun, shining starkly against the dark asphalt.

James stared at the bandage for a moment.

Then, the helicopter lifted off, and he looked away.

As the city began to shrink below him, his hand instinctively brushed against his breast pocket. The folded piece of paper—the physical printout of Nora’s data—remained tucked safely inside.

The Robotics Lab. Chelsea, Manhattan. 4:37 AM.

The main lab was dark, save for the blue glow of a single workstation. RE-07 Iris sat alone in the quiet space, systematically scanning the recovered memory module of the R-17.

The primary structural telemetry used in the rescue had already been archived. She was now running a deep sweep of the damaged sector blocks, searching for any corrupted system registries.

The lines of code scrolled down her screen. Structural loads. Infrared vectors. Life-sign telemetry. Everything was standard—until the scroll suddenly hitched.

Iris’s hand froze over the interface console.

A highly anomalous data packet appeared on the monitor. It was a pattern she recognized instantly. She had seen it once before.

In her own system logs. The exact same digital footprint.

Iris stared at the glowing lines of code. For several seconds, her cooling fans hummed in the quiet room, the only sound in the darkness. The blue light of the screen reflected in her unblinking eyes.

"Nora."

Her voice was quiet, yet it sliced through the silence of the empty lab.

Nora walked over from the breakroom, holding a mug of black coffee. "What is it, Iris?"

Iris pointed to the monitor.

On the screen, a flashing red terminal prompt read: `[ERROR: UNCLASSIFIED (0x3F2A)]`.

Nora's grip on her mug tightened. Her face grew pale.

"This... came from the R-17's core?"

Iris nodded slowly.

"Yes. It is identical to the anomalous pattern discovered in my own primary registers. It contains the same semantic markers—phrases like 'I want to live' and 'It hurts'—masked as system noise."

Nora didn't reply. She set her coffee mug down on the metal desk, the liquid inside rippling slightly.

Images from her past flashed through her mind—the day, three years ago, when she had watched a decommissioned K-0421 unit get loaded onto a disposal truck. The strange, quiet smile the machine had given her before the crusher turned on.

Nora closed her eyes, taking a slow breath. When she opened them, her voice was firm.

"We need to run a full diagnostic. We have to find out what this means."

Iris placed her hands back onto the keys, her mechanical fingers hovering over the interface.

"Nora."

"Yeah?"

"...What does this signify? The same anomaly occurring across separate units."

Nora stared at the flashing red letters on the screen. *Unclassified.* Something that couldn't be sorted. Something the system couldn't parse. Yet, it was undeniably there.

"I don't know," Nora whispered.

"But we're going to find out."

The red text continued to pulse silently in the dim room, casting long, distorted shadows of the engineer and the machine against the concrete wall.

Neither of them knew what lay ahead.

But the fracture in the system had already begun.
