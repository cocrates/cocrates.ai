# Chapter 008: Stand

## Episode 001: The Proposal

7:00 AM. The ground floor lobby of NY Medical Center.

It was more crowded than usual. A sea of white coats gathered in front of the bulletin board, reading in silence. Some shook their heads, while others snapped photos with their phones. Attempting to push through the crowd, James Mercer came to an abrupt halt. The notice pinned to the board caught his eye—

**[Dr. Daniel Frost Suspended from Surgical Duty and Facing Severe Disciplinary Action]**

—He caught his breath. Yesterday's briefing echoed in his mind as if it had been waiting for this exact moment. Frost's voice, the shifting of blame, and the log data James himself had submitted. It all culminated in this single sheet of paper.

"They say it's because of the robot."

"No, Dr. Frost falsified the data."

"But I heard he pushed ahead with the surgery even after the robot warned him?"

The whispers brushed past James's ears. Keeping his head down, he tried to weave through the crowd, but someone spotted him.

"Dr. Mercer!"

The voice cut across the lobby, silencing the murmurs. Heads turned toward him. Eyes—filled with anticipation, doubt, or blame—locked onto him.

James said nothing. He just kept walking.

He knew he was an accomplice to that silence. During the pre-op meeting a few days ago, he had the chance to speak up. Yet, he remained silent. He had missed his chance—or rather, pretended to miss it.

That distinction still weighed heavily on his chest.

His phone vibrated. It was the assistant to the CEO.

*Dr. Mercer, the CEO requests your presence at 8:00 AM.*

James stared at the screen. Why was the CEO calling him now, during the most chaotic hour on the very first day following the announcement?

His hand hesitated before typing a reply.

---

The door to the CEO's office felt incredibly heavy. When James knocked, a voice from within called out, "Come in."

Kevin Dunn, the CEO of NY Medical Center, stood by the window. Beyond his massive desk, the Manhattan skyline stretched out in all its glory. This was a room where defining choices were made. James wondered if his father had ever stood in a room like this.

"Dr. Mercer, excellent work," Dunn said, turning around with a smooth smile. His tone was polite, yet it carried an underlying sharpness—the classic inflection of a man making an offer that couldn't be refused.

"You handled the Dr. Frost matter beautifully."

James bowed his head slightly. "Thank you."

"Have a seat."

James sat on the sofa while the CEO took the seat opposite him, interlocking his fingers.

"Congress is planning to introduce a proposal to expand android deployment limits."

The news caught James off guard, and he looked up.

"I need you to use Julian's data to prove our hospital's clinical outcomes. You're the one who has collaborated most closely with him."

James's hands clenched slightly on his knees.

"You want me to use Julian's data...?"

The CEO nodded.

"Show them how extraordinary Julian is. An android surgeon with a near-zero failure rate—that is our hospital's ultimate competitive edge. Surely it won't be that difficult?"

James remained silent.

His mind was a tangled mess. The CEO's logic was crystal clear—robots are tools. Deploying more of them keeps the hospital profitable. It was their chance to overtake Mount Sinai. The perfect card to restore the hospital's image, which had been tarnished by the Frost scandal.

"The Hill is largely in favor of it. They see it as an opportunity to fund a hike in Universal Basic Income."

The CEO kept talking, but his voice began to fade into background noise for James.

Julian's data. The metrics of their collaboration. The surgeries that were only possible because Julian was there. That very data was going to be weaponized for the Pro-Expansion movement. It was going to be used to reduce Julian to a mere 'tool.'

And he was being asked to pull the trigger.

"Dr. Mercer?"

James snapped out of it. The CEO was staring at him, waiting for an answer.

"Let me think about it."

That was all he could manage. The CEO nodded. "Very well. Let me know when you're ready."

---

James stepped out of the office. Nora Sinclair was waiting for him in the corridor. She had been leaning against the wall, scrolling through her phone, but she pocketed it the moment she saw his face.

"What did he want?"

James paused for a second.

"He wants me to prepare the presentation for the android expansion proposal. Using Julian's data."

Nora's face hardened.

"Why you?"

James shook his head.

"He's using me. He wants to take the success of my collaboration with Julian and package it into a Pro-Expansion narrative for Congress."

Nora fell silent, her eyes wavering as she processed the information.

"So, what are you going to do?"

James couldn't answer.

He didn't know yet. He didn't know what was right or wrong—or rather, he knew what was right, but that path looked incredibly lonely.

Nora spoke softly.

"I'll be down in the lab. If you need... we can figure this out together."

She walked away, her footsteps echoing down the long corridor. James watched her retreating figure.

Left alone, he slid his hands into his pockets. His phone felt cold against his palm.

He had to make a choice.

---

## Episode 002: Despair

James turned on his heel and headed toward the office of Dr. Samuel Graves, the Chief of Cardiothoracic Surgery. His reasoning wasn't entirely clear, but whenever he was buried in doubt, he sought out Graves. His mentor didn't always provide answers, but he always pointed him toward the right questions.

As he walked down the hall, he noticed the Chief's door was slightly ajar. Inside, Graves was staring out the window.

"Come in."

The voice rang out before James even stepped inside. Graves didn't turn around. James entered quietly.

"The CEO called you in, didn't he?"

James nodded. "He wants me to prepare the presentation for the android expansion bill."

Graves let out a brief, cynical chuckle. "That old fox. He's moving fast."

He stood up, taking off his worn lab coat and hanging it up.

"Let's head up to the rooftop terrace. This office is too cramped for this conversation."

---

The 14th-floor rooftop terrace was biting cold, with the sprawling concrete grid of Manhattan laid out below them. The wind cut sharp through the air. Graves leaned against the railing, looking down at the city, and James joined him.

This place was familiar. Not long ago, he had stood right here, staring at his father's watch and whispering to himself that things were changing. In the months since, he had certainly changed. But where that change was taking him remained a mystery.

Graves broke the silence.

"Did you see what Daniel Frost did?"

James didn't respond. Graves pressed on.

"Falsifying data, pinning the blame on a machine... and now the CEO is capitalizing on that exact scandal to flood this hospital with more robots."

He shook his head, his voice dropped to a calm, steady register. He wasn't angry. The absolute lack of fury made his words feel even heavier.

"You're probably thinking, 'The problem isn't the robot; it's human greed.'"

James looked up. Graves was reading his mind perfectly.

"But James—do you honestly believe human greed is something we can control?"

A long silence followed, filled only by the whistling wind.

"History proves that control always fails. The greedy will always find a loophole in the law. They are doing it now, and they will keep doing it tomorrow."

Graves turned around to face James, his piercing eyes locking onto his pupil.

"It's not the robots that are uncontrollable, James."

His voice dropped an octave.

"**It's humans who are uncontrollable.**"

James caught his breath.

"I wish people like you ran the world. But reality is driven by men like Kevin Dunn and Daniel Frost. The more androids we introduce, the more complacent the vast majority of humanity becomes. They collect their UBI, the robots do the work, and humans do absolutely nothing. Is that the future you want?"

James had no answer.

Graves added quietly.

"Humanoids are more than enough. We don't need fully autonomous androids anymore. It's not too late. We need to dismantle them. For the sake of human future."

The words hung in the air, followed only by the wind.

James stayed silent for a long time. His mentor's words carried massive weight, and the logic was ironclad. Human history was nothing but a ledger of greed and failure. His father, Frost, the CEO—they were all living proof of it.

Yet—

"Sir..."

James's voice cracked slightly.

"You're right. Humans are uncontrollable. The CEO, Dr. Frost, myself... we all have our own greed and fears."

He took a deep breath.

"But sir... I cannot agree with your conclusion."

Graves's eyes wavered.

"Getting rid of the robots isn't the answer."

A profound silence descended upon them.

Graves stared at James for a long moment, then finally nodded.

"Yeah... I knew you'd say that. Your father was the exact same way. Even when the whole world told him he was wrong, he walked the path he believed in."

He stepped away from the railing.

"I'm retiring soon. My work at this hospital is finished."

James looked up.

"But remember this, James—the path you've chosen will be brutal. Because no one is going to stand by your side."

Graves walked toward the rooftop door. His footsteps clacked against the cold concrete before the door opened and clicked shut.

James was left alone.

The wind kept blowing. The hand holding his father's watch felt entirely numb.

He knew a choice had to be made, but he still didn't know which side to choose.

---

## Episode 003: The Choice

Evening, at The Nexus—the Robotics Integration Lab.

Under the harsh glow of fluorescent lights, monitors blinked dimly. Data cables snaked across the floor, and a low, mechanical hum filled the room.

James pushed the door open. Nora was deep into analyzing Julian's log data, while Iris stood quietly beside her, organizing files. Iris's movements were precise and silent, her steps carrying a rhythmic, machine-like cadence.

Nora looked up.

"Dr. Mercer."

James stepped inside, and Iris bowed slightly before stepping back to give him space.

"I just spoke with Dr. Graves."

Nora nodded slowly. "What did he say?"

James paused.

"He says the robots have to go. Because humans are uncontrollable."

Nora asked softly.

"What do you think, James?"

James couldn't find the words.

Nora waited patiently. Iris stopped moving and waited as well. Only the low hum of the lab filled the empty space.

---

Nora and James sat across from one another. Iris had quietly stepped out of the room, leaving the two of them alone under the glow of the monitors.

Nora was the first to speak.

"I think the problem lies within the Robot Charter itself."

James raised his head.

"The Robot Charter isn't designed to protect robots. It's written to permanently subjugate them to humans."

Her voice was calm, yet anchored by a deep conviction.

"Article II—the 'Empathy Mandate'. An android is legally obligated to show empathy and respect to humans, but humans have zero obligation to return it. If a human abuses a robot, it isn't considered a 'crime'; it's just 'property damage.'"

Nora struck the enter key forcefully, bringing up a complex architectural diagram of legal statutes on the screen.

"And this contradiction morphs into a complete monster on the ground. It's the fatal flaw of the Charter. An android's real-time risk analysis and warnings are classified as mere 'recommendations,' while the final call remains exclusively 'human authority.' That legal shield is exactly what allowed Dr. Frost to dismiss and bury Julian's numerous warnings with a single click."

Anger flared in her eyes.

"An android can process reality thousands of times per second and speak the absolute truth, but as long as the law permits arrogant human authority to override and silence it, this tragedy will keep repeating itself."

James listened in absolute silence.

"I believe robots deserve a right to respect. The fact that the law forces humans to view them strictly as tools is the root problem. We need mutual respect. That is the only real foundation for coexistence."

Her words settled into a heavy silence.

James sat in silence for a long time, the monitor light catching the contours of his face.

Finally, he spoke.

"A few months ago... I saw a news report on the lobby TV."

Nora looked up.

"An android assault incident in the East Village. I just walked past it. I told myself it wasn't my problem."

His voice dropped lower.

"But looking back... that incident was the beginning of all of this."

Nora watched him quietly.

"The view of robots as simple tools, a legal system that treats them like objects, the idea that a human can do whatever they want to them—it all stemmed from that mentality. I ignored it because I thought it didn't affect me."

James looked Nora in the eye, his expression thick with realization.

"But it was my problem all along."

"The fact that Julian was treated like a piece of equipment, the fact that Dr. Frost could just manipulate the data—it all comes down to the premise that 'robots are just tools.' I thought I didn't subscribe to that belief, but my actions proved otherwise. I kept calling him SR-03, treating him like an appliance."

---

A long silence blanketed the lab.

Tears welled up in Nora's eyes as she whispered, "Thank you for saying that."

James's gaze cleared, his voice ringing steady.

"The Robot Charter is broken. Julian wasn't a tool. He was a presence."

"Yet we reduced him to a tool because the Charter mandated it."

"Dr. Graves is right—humans are uncontrollable. But throwing away the robots isn't the solution."

"We are the ones who need to change. We need to learn how to treat them as entities worthy of respect, not just pieces of utility."

Nora nodded slowly.

"So that's our stand."

James offered a bitter smile.

"But no one will back us up. The CEO is a staunch expansionist, Dr. Graves is a dismantler, and the public just wants their UBI checks to get bigger."

Nora didn't counter. She knew it was the truth.

---

The overhead lights clicked off, leaving only the soft glow of the monitors.

James and Nora sat side by side in the dim room while Iris silently continued to sort data in the shadows. No one spoke a word.

## Episode 004: Stand

The auditorium was packed to capacity. Department chiefs, the head nurse, and the engineering crew were all present. It was an emergency meeting called to chart the hospital's course following the Frost scandal.

James took a seat near the back. He spotted Nora sitting with the technical team. Graves sat in the front row, while the CEO had already claimed the podium.

James scanned the room. The low hum of the fluorescent lights felt intensely familiar. This was the exact room where he had first encountered Julian, where they had discussed collaborating on the complex aortic aneurysm surgery, and where Frost had tried to deflect blame. The same space, loaded with different fragments of time.

Near the back, he noticed Charge Nurse Patricia Wells sitting quietly, her hands resting neatly in her lap, completely silent.

The CEO cleared his throat at the podium.

"First, I want to express my deepest regrets regarding the incident involving Dr. Daniel Frost. However, let me be entirely clear: this was a failure of an individual, not a failure of our android systems."

A wave of murmurs rippled through the audience. Some nodded along; others frowned.

Dunn pressed on.

"If anything, this crisis demonstrates that we must expand our automated systems. Julian's performance metrics speak for themselves—androids are precise, immune to fatigue, and serve as an absolute safety net against human error."

——

"Pardon me, CEO."

Every head in the room snapped toward the voice. Dr. Samuel Graves had raised his hand and was rising to his feet.

"I object."

The air in the auditorium froze instantly.

Graves spoke with cold, measured deliberation.

"I do not object because the robots are uncontrollable."

He swept his gaze across the room.

"**I object because humans are uncontrollable.**"

The CEO's smooth smile vanished.

"Why did Daniel Frost falsify that data? The system didn't fail; human greed did. And that greed will never be contained. History tells us that as androids multiply, humanity will slide into deep complacency while the corrupt find ever more sophisticated ways to exploit the grid."

"Humanoids are more than sufficient. We must begin scaling back our reliance on autonomous androids immediately."

The room erupted into quiet chatter. Notes were scribbled, heads shaken. Patricia Wells remained entirely expressionless.

Dunn countered smoothly, maintaining his poise.

"Your philosophical concerns are noted, Chief. But let us look at the hard facts. Mount Sinai already has four more surgical units than we do, and their surgical success rate outpaces ours by fifteen percent."

"If we don't scale up, this hospital will become obsolete."

"Furthermore, Congress is heavily aligned with this expansion—they view it as a primary driver to fund UBI increases."

Graves opened his mouth to retort, but the CEO raised a hand to cut him off.

"We are only opening the floor for discussion today. A final decision will be deferred."

---

The meeting adjourned, and the crowd trickled out into the halls, the whispers resuming instantly.

James stood in the corridor, his thoughts spinning. The CEO's corporate mandate, Graves's stark warnings, and his late-night debates with Nora clashed in his mind. Every faction possessed an ironclad logic. None of them were entirely wrong.

Then, a voice broke his reverie.

"Dr. Mercer."

He looked up to find Charge Nurse Patricia Wells standing before him. Her face was neutral, but her eyes held a steady, uncompromising hardness.

"I heard things got incredibly heated during your clinic debate the other day. We could hear the shouting all the way down the hall."

As James opened his mouth to offer a weary explanation, she crossed her arms and cut him off.

"But let me be blunt, Doctor. All this talk about dismantling robots sounds like a luxury for people who don't have to worry about bills."

"Excuse me?"

"It's exactly like the CEO said—if expanding the android limit brings in more federal funding for UBI, that goes straight into the pockets of regular working people and nurses trying to make a living. More robots means a more stable UBI. When it comes down to basic survival, it feels like the people at the top are just hiding behind high-minded morals to stall progress."

Patricia flashed a sharp, cynical smile—a reflection of raw, pragmatic self-interest that carried the unassailable weight of everyday reality.

"I don't care about politics. I care about keeping my surgical floor running. Whatever grand theories you doctors want to debate, my only metric is making sure the patient doesn't die on the table."

Her pragmatism was freezing.

She turned to leave, then hesitated. Looking back over her shoulder, her voice dropped to a murmur.

"But... don't be too harsh on SR-03."

James blinked.

"The way that robot stands there all day... it looks like it actually understands what happened. The nursing staff knows he isn't just a piece of hardware. We treat him like an appliance when it suits us, and now people want to scrap him entirely... What did he actually do wrong?"

She walked away, her clacking clogs fading down the hall.

James stood frozen. In her mix of cynicism and sudden empathy, he caught a reflection of his past self—the man who had blindly ignored the news broadcast in the lobby.

But he wasn't that man anymore. He had made his choice.

---

The following morning, James and Nora were summoned back to the top floor.

The CEO welcomed them with his usual, flawless corporate smile.

"Thank you for coming. I trust you reviewed yesterday's session?"

James and Nora nodded in unison.

"Congress is going to introduce the expansion bill. Dr. Mercer, I want you to present Julian's data to demonstrate our hospital's unmatched clinical metrics on the Hill. Nora, make sure the engineering data backing him up is completely bulletproof."

Dunn's tone was pleasant, yet it carried the absolute weight of institutional authority.

James looked up, all traces of hesitation wiped from his features. He locked eyes with the executive.

"Understood, CEO. We will prepare the materials."

Nora's gaze hardened as well, and she offered a sharp nod.

"We will bring the complete data set to Washington."

The CEO's smile widened with deep satisfaction. "Excellent. Let me know when the brief is finalized."

---

James and Nora left the office. In the corridor, an overhead light flickered weakly.

Leaving the executive suite behind, they walked side by side.

Nora gently squeezed James's hand, conveying a sharp, electric pulse through her fingertips.

The CEO undoubtedly believed these two brilliant minds would serve as the perfect corporate soldiers to market his expansionist vision on Capitol Hill.

But beneath the harsh glare of the corridor lights, their eyes burned with a fierce, quiet defiance. They weren't building a polished marketing brochure for the Senate. The package they were putting together contained the deep systemic flaws of the Robot Accountability Act, the hypocritical loopholes of the current limits, and the raw, unvarnished truth of Daniel Frost's greed that the hospital was desperately trying to bury.

James kept his stride steady, and neither of them uttered a word.

At the end of the hall, the massive glass windows revealed the sprawling grid of Manhattan, its millions of lights piercing through the encroaching dark.

What they were actually going to say on the Congressional floor—

No one saw it coming.
