# Chapter 010: Value

## Episode 001: The Gavel

The Senate HELP Committee room.

Fourteen nameplates rested solemnly on the expansive mahogany horseshoe desk. James traced each name with his eyes: Harrison Hale, Christopher Hayes, Thomas Porter, Brian Walsh, Steven Hart, Joel Drake, Nathan Cole, Robert Crawford, Claire Bennett, Sean Blake, David Crane, Paul Donovan, Karen Mitchell. The list Nora had sent him the night before had taken human form, staring down at him from the dais.

Seated at the witness table below them, James and Nora felt tiny against the grandeur of the room.

"The committee will come to order," Chairman Harrison Hale announced, his dry, practiced legal tone echoing through the microphone. He brought the gavel down twice, instantly silencing the room.

"Today we convene to consider the automated deployment caps for the next fiscal year. We will begin with testimony from Dr. James Mercer of NY Medical Center."

James stood up, a chill rising through his shoes as he took his place.

"Thank you, Mr. Chairman."

James activated the display projector, filling the screen with Julian's surgical metrics. The numbers were immaculate: over 200 flawless operations, a 99.2% success rate, and absolutely zero post-operative complications.

"Since his deployment, SR-03 has maximized surgical efficiency on our floor. The real-world data demonstrates—"

"A moment, Dr. Mercer," Brian Walsh interrupted. Sitting on the aggressive left flank of the panel, his lips curved into a patronizing smile.

"We are all well aware of the pristine metrics. But I see a distinct contradiction here. Weren't you originally one of the most vocal opponents of clinical automation? The record shows you stood firmly alongside the nursing unions and Dr. Graves when the initiative launched."

James's hands tightened. Walsh was aiming directly at his credibility.

"What changed? Did your CEO order you to present this pro-expansion narrative?"

"My understanding of the technology changed," James countered, fighting to keep his tone even.

"SR-03 proved to be far more than a processing unit. I witnessed him make real, existential decisions in the operating room to prioritize human life."

"Existential decisions? A fascinating poetic flourish," Walsh scoffed.

"But an android is merely an advanced utility. At Mount Sinai, we have successfully managed and governed these assets for three years. It seems NY Medical Center is only now recognizing their value, but had you put your professional pride aside sooner, your patients would have benefited much earlier."

James felt his fingers twitch. Walsh's pragmatism was cutthroat. In his mind, James saw the log files of Julian's deviation.

*He bypassed protocols and risked deletion to save a human life.*

Should he reveal it now? Should he scream that Julian wasn't just equipment?

He glanced toward Nathan Cole at the far end of the dais. The Atlas Dynamics CEO was watching him with a cold, calculating stare. James knew the corporate math: if he admitted Julian had acted outside his core parameters, Cole would immediately label the unit a rogue asset and order him scrapped.

To defend Julian's humanity meant signing his death warrant.

Trapped in the corporate snare, James swallowed the truth. He had no choice but to rely on the counter-arguments of Dr. Crawford to bail him out.

"Clinical utility is not the same as human replacement," James stated, keeping Julian's secrets hidden.

"Medicine is fundamentally built on the human relationship, not just numerical optimization."

"Gentlemen, reserve your debate for the open floor," Chairman Hale interjected, bringing the gavel down once more.

James sank back into his seat, his palms slick with sweat. He couldn't tell if his silence had protected Julian or merely proven his own cowardice.

## Episode 002: Testimony

"The chair recognizes Ms. Nora Sinclair, lead systems engineer at NY Medical Center."

Nora rose slowly, her knuckles white against the edge of her tablet.

"Thank you, Mr. Chairman."

Nora steadied her breath and pulled up the architectural log files for CN-04, Mia. The monitors filled with crimson warning lines and a dense cascade of unclassified error codes.

"Over the past eight months in the palliative care ward, CN-04 has logged exactly 3,472 unclassified exceptions. These are not random mechanical glitches. They represent severe system overloads caused by the continuous accumulation of emotional data during end-of-life care. This is evidence that the android is undergoing severe cognitive distress—"

*Tap.*

"Ms. Sinclair, that is enough," Chairman Hale cut in, tapping his pen against the desk.

"This line of testimony is entirely irrelevant to the economic and logistical efficiency parameters of the expansion bill."

"It is completely relevant!" Nora's voice flared with desperate conviction.

"The degradation of this architecture under emotional stress poses a direct threat to patient safety in clinical environments! This addresses the core 'mental health' of the asset—"

"That is a matter for the manufacturer's technical support, not the United States Senate."

Hale's voice remained perfectly polite, yet completely unyielding. He gestured to the technician in the booth.

"Clear the witness's display."

Before Nora could protest, the graphs of Mia's internal suffering vanished from the screen, replaced by total darkness. It was a clinical blackout.

"Mr. Chairman! Mia is breaking down right now!"

"Ms. Sinclair, this committee regulates commerce; we do not provide therapy for machines."

His words cut sharp through the room.

Nora stared blankly at the dark screen, her lips tight as she endured the indifferent stares of the panel.

Then, Dr. Sean Blake from MIT activated his microphone.

"Mr. Chairman, Ms. Sinclair's point is technically sound. The core architecture designed by Atlas Dynamics intentionally locks emotional data processing. Unprocessed inputs stack up internally until they destabilize the OS. Is Atlas Dynamics deliberately hiding this architectural flaw?"

The room turned to Nathan Cole.

The corporate titan didn't flinch. He sat with his hands folded, staring ahead with complete composure, his only concern being how this disclosure might impact the company's stock value.

"...The data presented remains unverified," Cole stated flatly.

A single sentence, followed by total silence. The corporate defense was impenetrable, leaving the room without any legal leverage to challenge him.

## Episode 002.5: Beyond the Room

While the debate inside the committee room centered on ethics and efficiency, the world outside was already beginning to boil.

On the monitors in the waiting lounge, the live digital streams were updating every second. The competing tags `#RobotsAreTools` and `#MachinesFeelPain` clashed fiercely online, while news networks broadcast live footage of over three hundred Dismantle Now protesters gathering outside the Capitol gates.

—

Meanwhile, back at NY Medical Center.

Standing by a digital kiosk in the lobby, Patricia Wells watched the live stream on her phone, letting out a sharp chuckle.

"More automation means lower medical bills and a higher UBI dividend. Why are those people in Washington wasting time over machine feelings?"

A passing floor nurse paused, her expression uneasy.

"I don't know, Patricia... Have you looked at the androids lately? Something about them feels off."

Patricia pocketed her phone. "Off? They're machines, what could be off?"

"Mia, down in palliative care," the nurse whispered, leaning closer.

"She follows her programming perfectly, smiling at the patients... but her eyes look completely hollow. Yesterday, after a patient passed away, she just stood by the bed in total silence for thirty minutes. It looked... like she was screaming inside."

"Screaming inside...?"

A sudden wave of doubt hit Patricia. She remembered the blacked-out data screen from the broadcast.

*If these machines are actually suffering under the weight of our demands... what does that make the UBI money we collect every month?*

Shaking off the thought, she forced a dismissive wave.

"Nonsense. It's just a software error. I have a floor to manage."

She walked away, but her trembling fingers betrayed her composure.

—

At the same time, an ominous manifesto was circulating through the darker corners of the web.

> **[MANIFESTO: We will tear down the automated cages that devalue human existence. Tonight, we strike at the machine's heart.]**
> 

*Bzzzz.*

Deep in the committee room, James felt his phone vibrate aggressively against his leg. He pulled it out under the desk to see a red alert from the hospital's security network.

`[CRITICAL: Abnormal traffic and sudden thermal spike detected in Basement Level 2 Data Center. External breach or physical intrusion suspected.]`

James went pale. The storm was breaking.

## Episode 003: The Frame War

3:00 PM. The hearing resumed.

The mood in the room had grown suffocatingly tense. James was distracted by the security warnings from New York, but the political gears kept grinding.

Professor David Crane opened the floor.

"We are not here to debate metrics; we are here to protect the human soul. If we let efficiency dictate our society, we erase human dignity. This is an existential crisis, not a balance sheet."

Then, Professor Claire Bennett stepped forward, holding a copy of the Robot Charter.

"Article II states that an android must show respect and empathy to humans," she noted, dropping the text onto the desk.

"Who gave us the right to mandate empathy from a machine?"

The room fell completely silent.

"Real empathy requires free will. A forced program is nothing but automated hypocrisy. We demand a moral standard from machines that we fail to meet ourselves, all to preserve our own comfort."

She delivered her final line with absolute certainty.

"Forced empathy is violence."

The words hit James hard, cutting right to the core of Julian's silent struggle.

But Joel Drake of DataPulse quickly countered, pulling up his polling metrics.

"A beautiful sentiment, Professor, but the public responds to entirely different frames. Look at the data. Frame this issue around morality, and 31% object to expansion. But shift the narrative to 'economic growth and medical efficiency,' and support surges to 47%."

Drake let out a cold smile.

"Philosophy doesn't pay the bills. People follow the frame that benefits them."

"So this entire hearing is just a corporate performance," Karen Mitchell muttered from the right flank, her bitter remark picking up on the open mic.

"Our values mean nothing here; the financial outcome was decided long before we sat down. It's a farce."

---

6:00 PM. The first session concluded.

The panel dispersed quickly. Walsh left while talking animatedly on his phone, and Cole exited silently, carrying his brief like a fortress.

Only three figures remained in the empty room.

Nora stared blankly at the dark projection screen, the shadow of Mia's distress still lingering in her eyes.

"James... did our testimony actually help him?"

James gripped his still-vibrating phone tightly, the red warning lights reflecting against his skin.

"I don't know..."

"The record has been saved," Iris said, stepping forward with her terminal. Her mechanical eyes held the complete logs of human conflict, yet her voice carried an eerie, profound sadness.

"But none of the numbers on that mahogany desk captured the true reality of Julian or Mia."

As Iris powered down her unit, the remaining lights in the room clicked off one by one.

James looked out the grand windows. The sun was setting over Washington, casting long, ominous shadows across the city.

Bennett's warning kept echoing in his mind.

*—It is violence.*

He checked his screen again, the red alert still blinking relentlessly. The crisis had already arrived at their doorstep.
