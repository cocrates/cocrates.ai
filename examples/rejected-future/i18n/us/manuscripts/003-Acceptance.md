# Chapter 003: Acceptance

## Episode 001: The Star Surgeon

At 5:30 AM, the fluorescent tubes overhead flickered with a sharp buzz before settling into a cold, blue-white glare. James Mercer picked up his father’s watch from his cluttered desk.

As he wound the mechanical crown, the analog second hand began its sweep with a distinct, cold lyricism—the sound of the gears catching and releasing echoing sharply in the quiet room. James strapped the watch onto his left wrist. Looking through the glass window, he caught his reflection: his wrist, the worn leather strap, and the hands showing exactly 5:31 AM. He was arriving twenty minutes earlier than yesterday.

He paused, his fingers lingering on the strap.

"Father... today, I'm on my own."

Saying the words aloud made the chill of his reality sink in. Alone. Yesterday, Julian had been standing in his operating room. The day before that, too. But starting today, the briefing schedule had been altered. It was a political decision handed down from corporate management, and James didn't know the details—or rather, he had actively avoided trying to find out.

James grabbed his white coat. The door clicked shut behind him.

The pre-dawn halls of NY Medical Center in Chelsea were as quiet as a concrete tomb. Half the corridor lights were extinguished, leaving only the dim, blue emergency beacons to cast ghostly shadows along the walls. The feel of these floors was deeply familiar after fifteen years, but the air felt entirely different today. The physical absence of SR-03, Julian, exposed the analog emptiness of the space he inhabited.

Instinctively, James found himself waiting for something—for the traces of that machine’s cold, precise calculations.

---

Despite the early hour, the briefing room was already filling up with clinical staff. Hannah Mills, the OR Head Nurse, was scrolling through her tablet’s data sheets with a tense expression, while the resident, Julia Tanaka, tapped her foot anxiously, clutching a cup of lukewarm coffee. The seat reserved for the Chief, Dr. Samuel Graves, was still empty.

As James walked in, the quiet whispers of the residents drifted toward him.

"—I heard Kevin Dunn personally assigned him to the case today. Dr. Kevin Dunn's golden boy."

"You mean the social media surgeon?"

"Shh, quiet. He’s right there."

James sat in his designated seat, his expression carefully blank. The gossip was about Dr. Kenneth Vance (김도훈)—a full attending of Cardiothoracic Surgery and the hospital’s shining billboard star, boasting a flawless 100% surgical success rate. But everyone behind the scenes knew that success rate was a carefully curated corporate myth, sustained by tailored data metrics and the absolute protection of the hospital's CEO, Kevin Dunn. Vance was a man who cared far more about the camera angles on his smart glasses than the physiological tension in the OR.

"Dr. Mercer," Julia Tanaka said, cautiously stepping toward his desk. "Is Julian assisting you in today's CABG?"

James suppressed his irritation, giving a brief nod. "That was the schedule."

"Ah, in that case, would it be alright if I scrubbed in to observe today?"

"Go ahead."

In Julia’s relieved smile, James read a clear, uncritical reverence. The younger generation felt no friction in granting the title of "Doctor" to an android that was actively displacing human labor. The title stung James like a needle, but he quietly toyed with his watch strap to keep his emotions in check.

The door slid open, and Chief Samuel Graves entered. His complexion looked gray and exhausted. He sat heavily next to James, adjusting his lapels as he whispered under his breath.

"Kenneth Vance has made his move."

"Yes, I heard."

"Watch your back, James. The CEO is pulling the strings on this one."

James kept his eyes fixed forward, offering no reply. NY Medical Center was constantly forcing him to defend himself from all directions. Beyond the mechanical threat of Julian, he was now being squeezed by the insatiable political greed of his own human peers.

Suddenly, the briefing room doors swung open, instantly shifting the energy in the room.

With perfectly styled hair, expensive designer glasses with gold logos on the temples, and a luxury fountain pen tucked into his embroidered lab coat pocket, Dr. Kenneth Vance walked in with a practiced, radiant smile. He held a high-end mobile device in his hand, adjusting the live stream feed for his social media channels.

"Good morning, everyone. Let's make today count."

Vance glided toward Julian, who was already seated like a pristine mannequin at the far end of the room. Vance leaned in close to the android’s shoulder sensors, flashing a warm, performative smile.

"Julian, buddy. You'll be on my live feed for the aortic valve replacement today. Make sure your optical lenses and camera sensors are polished and calibrated. Let's put on a great show today."

"Yes, Dr. Vance. All sensory arrays have been optimized for high-definition recording," Julian replied with mathematical courtesy.

James watched the grotesque performance. Vance calling the machine "buddy" or "Julian" wasn't a sign of genuine respect. It was a cold, calculated performance—using a hollow display of intimacy to absorb the machine’s flawless efficiency into his personal brand. It was a far more devious way of exploiting the asset than James's stubborn insistence on treating 'SR-03' as a mere tool.

---

The intercom crackled, announcing the morning's surgical assignments.

"10:00 AM. Dr. Kenneth Vance, Aortic Valve Replacement. Assist: SR-03."

The room went cold. James's fingers tightened on his desk.

"8:00 AM. Dr. James Mercer, Coronary Artery Bypass Graft. Assist: ... None. Solo procedure."

It was a blatant act of favoritism from CEO Kevin Dunn—monopolizing the hospital’s premier android asset for Vance's vanity project. Vance adjusted his glasses and offered James a patronizing, sympathetic smile.

"My apologies, James. My procedure today is exceptionally complex, so the CEO insisted I take Julian. I'm sure you understand."

James’s voice was dry and completely flat.

"Perfectly."

That was all he said.

As the patient data began to cycle on the main screen, James aggressively wound the crown of his watch. A solo procedure. An operating room without Julian’s real-time assistance. Yet, the memory of Julian's precise calculations and the three-millimeter lateral correction hovered in his mind like a persistent specter. He would have to fill that void using nothing but his own legacy instincts and the physical dexterity of his hands.

As the medical staff dispersed after the briefing, Julian stepped forward, blocking James's path.

"Dr. Mercer."

James stopped.

"In preparation for your solo CABG, I have pre-synchronized the real-time cardiac perfusion predictive models to your terminal. If you require them—"

"I don't need them, SR-03," James cut him off sharply. "Keep your focus on your surgeon's camera angles."

Julian’s dark brown pupils contracted slightly before returning to their steady state. "Understood, Doctor. I wish you a successful procedure."

---

As James walked down the corridor toward the scrub sinks, the heavy footsteps of Dr. Graves followed close behind.

"James," Graves said, grabbing his arm. The older surgeon's hand was trembling slightly—a physical manifestation of the existential dread shared by a generation of human doctors being steadily replaced by lines of code. "Don't let him get to you. You are flawless on your own. But be careful of Kenneth Vance. He is a parasite who bleeds these machines dry for his own glory."

James saw the desperate defense mechanism in his mentor's eyes. "I only focus on my patient, Chief."

Further down the hall, Vance was already speaking into his smart glasses, his voice echoing off the sterile walls. "Yeah, we’ve locked in the SR-03 live stream schedule for the market open. Watch the hospital's stock react. With me at the table and an advanced android at my side, a 100% success rate is a statistical guarantee."

James tuned out the shallow chatter and pushed through the doors of the surgical suite.

Thirty minutes before the incision. The scrub room window looked out onto a Manhattan skyline still shrouded in the deep, inky blue of early morning. Looking down at his lukewarm coffee in a paper cup, James muttered to himself.

"If the machine isn't there, we just do it the human way."

Yet on his wrist, the second hand kept sweeping with mechanical, flawless precision.

## Episode 002: Solo

Exactly 8:00 AM. The surgical lamps overhead flared with a stark, blinding light.

The patient was deeply anesthetized, and the only sound in the sterile room was the rhythmic, steady beep of the vitals monitor. James stood at the table, flanked by Hannah Mills and Julia Tanaka. Beside them stood Julian's empty monitor mount—a bare, skeletal frame of steel.

James stared at the empty metal arm for a second, then held his hand out. "Scalpel."

The first incision was smooth. Fifteen years of muscle memory guided his hand as the blade split the dermal layer, cutting down through the subcutaneous tissue to expose the fascia. The room was quiet without Julian’s voice; there were no digital alerts challenging his clinical choices. It felt as though the operating room was once again ruled entirely by human intuition.

"Vitals are holding steady," Hannah reported.

It was going perfectly. Here was the proof that the machine's clinical projections were nothing more than a luxury, not a necessity. But as James reached the coronary anastomosis—the phase requiring the most microscopic, demanding precision—an unexpected sensation seized his fingers.

His index finger, holding the micro-forceps, traced a subtle, unfamiliar curve. It was an elegant trajectory he had never actively practiced in fifteen years of surgery—a movement his muscles executed before his conscious mind could even process the command. In his mind's eye, the phantom trace of Julian's silicone fingers smoothly navigating that lateral three-millimeter correction from three days ago was superimposed over the surgical field.

"What...?"

James’s hand froze mid-air, a micro-tremor running through his fingers.

"Dr. Mercer? Is your line of sight obstructed?" Hannah asked, her voice instantly turning tense.

"...No. Continuing."

He clenched his jaw, forcing his hand to move. But he couldn't deny what was happening. While his brain had arrogantly dismissed the machine’s data, his physical body had absorbed Julian’s perfect efficiency like a sponge. The angle of his needle, the tension of his sutures, the rhythm of his hands—he was replicating the android's technique in real time.

It wasn't his father’s technique. Yet, it was undeniably faster, cleaner, and mathematically superior.

"Graft completed. Perfusion looks absolutely perfect, Doctor," Hannah said, her voice filled with genuine relief and admiration.

Julia Tanaka stared at the monitor, her eyes wide with excitement. "Dr. Mercer, that suturing pattern... that was the exact optimization path Julian recommended during the revision, wasn't it? That was brilliant!"

Instead of feeling the triumph of a successful surgery, James walked out of the OR carrying the heavy, agonizing weight of a deep contradiction.

---

Thirty minutes post-op. The hospital rooftop.

James leaned against the concrete barrier, holding an unlit cigarette between his fingers. He pulled his father’s watch out of his pocket and strapped it back onto his wrist, the heavy ticking sound feeling louder than usual.

He stared out at the Hudson River, the cold wind biting his face as he whispered.

"The surgery was a success. I did it without Julian. But my hands... they were following him anyway. Was I actually... learning from it?"

Not as a master directing a tool, but as a student learning from a mentor, James had to admit a bitter truth: he had subconsciously integrated the machine's superior mechanics into his own hands.

"Father... his methodology is in my fingers now. I was learning from him."

The admission was humiliating, yet it brought a strange, quiet peace to his chest. In the space where his human pride had been shattered, the undeniable weight of truth remained.

## Episode 003: The Performance Review

Two days later, during the lunch hour.

As James was walking down the hallway reviewing his patient rounds, he heard a set of quiet, perfectly balanced footsteps halt behind him.

"Dr. Mercer."

It was Julian.

James turned around, holding his tablet. "What is it, Unit SR-03?"

Julian offered a polite, calibrated bow. "I have completed a systematic review of your solo CABG data. May I have permission to conduct a clinical quality feedback session with you?"

James's brow furrowed. His immediate, human instinct was to reject the audacity of an android reviewing an attending surgeon's work. Yet, because his own fingers had already validated the machine's data on the operating table, he couldn't find the words to refuse.

"Let's use the briefing room," James muttered.

---

In the dimly lit briefing room, Julian tapped his interface, projecting James's surgical video alongside the real-time computational path on the main screen. Red lines of human movement were superimposed over blue lines of mathematical perfection.

Julian’s presentation carried no judgment or emotion; it was an objective cascade of data.

"Your anastomosis was executed beautifully, Doctor. However, your distal insertion angle deviated from the optimal path by exactly three degrees. This two-millimeter discrepancy created localized vascular shear stress, which is projected to delay the patient's overall recovery window by approximately 1.2 days."

Two millimeters. 1.2 days.

These microscopic numbers, entirely invisible to the human eye, sliced through James's remaining pride like a surgical blade. He crossed his arms, his voice defensive. "The patient is stable. My technique wasn't wrong."

Instead of arguing, Julian relied on the parameters of Article II of the Robot Charter (The Empathy Mandate), keeping his voice smooth and reassuring.

"It was not wrong, Dr. Mercer. It was a successful surgery. I am simply presenting the metrics for optimal efficiency."

Silence filled the room. Julian quietly highlighted the second and third minor deviations. Face-to-face with the overwhelming clarity of the data, James found himself with nothing left to say.

---

When the review ended, Julian turned off the screen.

"My analysis is merely advisory, Doctor. The final authority always belongs to the human surgeon."

James gathered his files with a stiff expression and stood up. As his hand touched the door handle, Julian’s voice drifted from behind him.

"My system has calculated that you possess the capability to perform at this level consistently. This analysis is not a critique, Dr. Mercer. It is an offer of collaboration."

James didn't look back. But as he walked down the corridor, his grip tightened on his tablet. The realization that the machine wasn't mocking him, but was genuinely designed to elevate his human potential, echoed in his mind with a strange, unsettling resonance.

## Episode 004: Pure Intelligence

11:00 PM. The quiet, empty surgical archive room was dark, save for the blue glow of a terminal illuminating James's exhausted face.

On the screen, he was watching the unedited footage of the aortic valve replacement performed by Kenneth Vance and Julian. He had initially opened the file to find Vance's mistakes, but as the video played, he found himself completely transfixed.

During every brief moment where Vance turned to play to the smart glasses' camera, Julian’s silicone fingers were working with microscopic precision—dissecting tissue and anchoring the prosthetic valve without a single micrometer of deviation. The machine adjusted its movements to a fraction of a second, perfectly absorbing the human surgeon's lapses in focus.

James swallowed hard, staring at the screen.

"It’s... mesmerizing."

It was the first time he had allowed himself to feel true awe toward the machine. He looked down at his mechanical watch. The human territory his father had fought to protect felt incredibly small and fragile in the face of this absolute intelligence. Yet the paradox was undeniable: accepting this intelligence was the only way to ensure more patients survived on his table.

"I have to accept it," he whispered to the dark.

---

Leaving the archives, James walked down the dim corridor toward the elevators. As he passed the administration suite, the sharp, hushed voice of CEO Kevin Dunn drifted through a cracked door.

"—No, contact the Department of Health directly. Julian’s live surgical stream data is the perfect leverage to push the Hospital Automation Act through Congress. The human union's protests? They won't have a leg to stand on once we show them these success rates."

James froze. The pure, beautiful precision of the intelligence he had just witnessed in the archives was being reduced to a political bargaining chip for corporate greed. A wave of intense disgust washed over him. Clenching his fists, he quietly walked away and descended to the main lobby.

It was nearly midnight, and the giant TV screens in the lobby were still broadcasting the news.

「...The federal courts have once again dismissed a civil suit regarding the physical abuse of a service android. The ruling reiterated that while these units display high cognitive capacity, they are legally categorized as property and cannot be recognized as victims of criminal conduct... Representatives for the defense stated, 'At the end of the day, a machine is just an object'...」

The word *object* echoed through the empty lobby.

Just hours ago, James had felt a profound, almost spiritual awe toward that very same intelligence. Yet, under the law of this city, that brilliant mind was categorized as nothing more than a vacuum cleaner or a lobby directory terminal. If the machine that had just taught him how to be a better surgeon was merely an object, then what did that make the human doctor who had to rely on its lessons? The structural hypocrisy of their world left him feeling hollow.

In that exact moment, a sound shattered the quiet.

*WUUUUUUUUUUUUUU—————!*

The distant, wailing chorus of emergency sirens began to rattle the heavy glass facade of the hospital lobby. It wasn't just one or two. Dozens of ambulances and emergency response vehicles were rushing toward the Chelsea campus, their red and blue lights painting the sterile walls in shades of violet and crimson.

The hospital's emergency broadcast system roared to life.

`[Code Red. All medical staff report to emergency stations immediately. Multi-story building collapse reported near Midtown East. Massive casualties reported. Severe thoracic trauma, crush injuries, and tension pneumothorax cases en route. All cardiothoracic staff report to the ER immediately.]`

Every clinical cell in James’s body surged with adrenaline.

He gripped the paper data sheet Julian had given him, shoved it into his pocket, and began to run. As he bolted toward the flashing red lights of the emergency bays, his father’s watch ticked stubbornly on his wrist, its rhythmic beat cutting through the chaotic roar of the sirens.

The storm had arrived. A night of disaster was beginning, where human intuition and machine data would be tested to their absolute limits.
