# Chapter 013: Terrorism

## Episode 001: The Assembly

The basement.

A single, aging fluorescent bulb flickered precariously. The low, 60Hz electrical hum of the utility lines filled the cold air with a heavy, oppressive static. Moisture seeped from the concrete walls—walls that held no blind spots—weighing down the very atmosphere.

Kevin Cross sat before a raw steel table. His phone, which had just fallen silent, was still gripped in his hand.

The phantom warmth of the machine lingered against his palm.

Karen Mitchell’s voice, which had just echoed through the receiver, was imprinted in his mind like a compass needle.
*“It’s passed. You were right, Kevin. We have to change our methods.”*

Kevin set the phone down slowly. He stood up and began to pace the floor. His leather shoes struck the rough concrete, echoing with a dull, heavy finality.

*Change the method.*

He had spent an eternity mentally designing what that method should look like. But now, as his hand hovered over the trigger, a fleeting moment of hesitation shackled his fingertips.

He checked his watch. There was still time before the others arrived.

On the steel table lay a copy of John Locke’s *Two Treatises of Government*, dog-eared since his college days. It was open to the page on the "right to resistance"—the final authority of the people when a government betrays its trust.

Back then, he had read those words as an intellectual exercise. *What if I lived in an age of barbarism where the law remained silent? What choice would I make?*

Now, he was standing in the dead center of that exact age. Inside the halls of the U.S. Capitol in D.C., a sophisticated violence had been legalized under the guise of progress. And here, in this damp, dark basement in Bushwick, he was planning an equally cold, uncompromising counter-strike. The weight of both decisions was identical, but the methods were worlds apart.

Kevin snapped the book shut. The time for elegant theories was over.

He walked toward a large map of New York City pinned to the warehouse wall. Several key locations were marked in bold red ink: NY Medical Center, the U.S. Capitol, and the Atlas Dynamics headquarters.

His finger traced over the Atlas Dynamics building before sliding away.

Above, the heavy steel door creaked open. Footsteps followed.

The first arrival.

---

Ian "Raven" Black, the information and communications specialist, hurried down the stairs. His ragged breathing broke the basement’s silence. His eyes burned with the intensity of someone who had unearthed a critical lead.

Without even a nod to Kevin, Raven headed straight for the communications nook.

"I found it," Raven muttered. "It’s worse than we thought."

Hank "Gear" Olson, the robotics engineer, entered the basement next. As always, he kept his head bowed, stopping at the threshold of the workbench. Habitually, he rubbed his calloused hands against his pants, cleaning off invisible grease from the mechanical components he spent his life fixing. Even though his hands were clean, the ritual was constant. After a brief pause, Gear slipped into the workshop and began silently organizing his scattered tools.

Kevin watched them both.

Raven, chasing information, was too fast. Gear, lost in the machinery, was too slow. Yet, both distinct paths led to the same destination.

Raven emerged from the communications nook and spread several printouts across the steel table. They were grainy, black-and-white images of an architectural core analysis. Kevin understood the implications immediately, but he held his tongue.

"It begins soon," Raven said. The warning turned the air in the room brittle.

---

Breaking the heavy silence, Henry Nash walked down the steps.

He exchanged a fleeting, wordless glance with Kevin. The sheer stillness radiating from his massive frame made the basement’s intensity climb an octave. Nash didn't move; he simply leaned against the far wall. His blank gaze began to scan the room with surgical precision: Kevin’s expression, the briefcase Maya Preston had left, the photos on Raven’s desk, and the damp hands of Gear standing in the doorway. As a recorder and a strategist, he was engraving every detail into his mind.

Maya Preston followed, clutching her briefcase tightly.

The case landed on the table with a heavy thud, as if filled with lead. She opened it to organize the files she had gathered: the draft for the Atlas Dynamics robot deployment legislation, the medical center’s staff-reduction plans, and the infrastructure expansion timeline to be implemented post-vote.

Kevin watched her methodical movements. The detail in her plans exceeded even his highest expectations.

"Are you ready?" Maya asked, lifting her head as she flattened a document.

"Only if you have made your decision."

The weight of their shared trust—or perhaps the sense of inevitable ruin—settled over Nash’s silence.

---

The final footsteps rumbled through the basement like a low-frequency pulse.

An exhausted, heavy gait. Kevin knew exactly who it was without looking.

Karen Mitchell, a former union organizer who had felt the full crushing weight of the system’s limitations from the gallery of the congressional hearings, descended into the basement. Her eyes, rimmed with red exhaustion, locked onto Kevin’s.

A spark of friction. 0.5 seconds.

She didn't offer a greeting, choosing a chair at the far end of the table, as far from Kevin as possible. Kevin recognized the psychological defense she was putting up. He knew better than anyone that she was trying to distance herself. He cleared his throat to speak, but Karen beat him to it.

"I haven't agreed to this madness yet. I’m just here to listen."

At her icy remark, the others turned their gaze toward Kevin.

---

Finally, all six sat around the steel table.

Kevin slowly rose to his feet. His shadow stretched long under the cold fluorescent light. He stared deep into each of their faces. This rundown, damp basement would become the gunpowder store for a new history.

He looked down at his palms.

They were steady. At least, to everyone else, they appeared that way.

He spoke, his voice rebounding off the damp concrete with immovable force.

"Comrades, today we must make a final, irreversible decision."

He left a deliberate silence.

"The elegant war of words and paper ended today with an 8-to-6 vote. The hearings were rigged, Congress was bought by corporate lobbying, and the Robot Charter has become a legal blade used to fatten the pockets of the elite. We have exhausted every peaceful avenue."

He looked directly at Karen.

"Now, we must answer the most fundamental question. Will we be trampled into submission, or will we respond in their own language?"

The fluorescent light flickered with a neurotic buzz. In that silence, no one dared to refute him. The dampness of the basement bore witness that the age of peace had officially come to an end.

## Episode 002: The Debate

"We have truly done everything we could."

Kevin leaned forward, his hands gripping the edge of the table.

"We presented data, argued with logic, and brought witnesses to appeal to their morality. But what was the result? They threw away our entire ethics with two words: 'National Competitiveness.' The Robot Accountability Act is nothing but a massive enclosure fence to legalize their greed."

He swallowed hard. His mouth felt like ash.

"Their frame was powerful. Security, competitiveness, technological supremacy. We fought with values, but they crushed us with force. There is only one solution. We must respond in the language of force."

Henry Nash gave a heavy nod of agreement, and Raven tapped the table in solidarity.

But Karen Mitchell shook her head firmly. Her chair screeched against the floor.

---

"Your logic is always so compelling, Kevin."

Karen stood up, firing back.

"But don't delude yourself. Just because peaceful methods failed doesn't make terrorism justified. Violence is the path of self-destruction that loses public support faster and more completely than anything else."

She brought up the painful history of the labor movements she had spent her life serving.

"Have you forgotten when we won and when we were crushed in the history of union strikes? The moment we lose public sympathy and are branded as a violent group, the law and capital get the moral pretext they need to hunt us down like stray dogs. No matter how noble the motive, the moment our methods are soaked in blood, the public will only remember us as psychotic terrorists."

Her voice trembled. It was proof that she, too, felt the agonizing frustration of the vote's outcome.

"The moment citizens turn their backs on us, we become nothing more than evil thugs who couldn't change a thing!"

"Then what?" Kevin shouted, enraged. "Do you want us to sit on our hands while they take our jobs and let those machines run us into the ground?"

As their emotions reached a breaking point, Henry Nash intervened quietly.

"You both fundamentally desire the same destination. You are only divided by the depth of the chasm between your methods."

---

Then, Raven, who had been leaning against the doorway, muttered low.

"If the sacrifice of the few... can prevent the ruin of the many, is that violence truly unjustified?"

There was a strange madness in his eyes. Kevin didn't look away.

"History has always changed its trajectory through the decisions of such few people."

Karen laughed in disbelief.

"That is exactly what greed is, Kevin! The very idea that the sacrifice of a few is acceptable is no different from the architecture of the system that sacrifices our workers under the guise of the 'National' interest! You are just becoming another monster trying to justify violence under the same empty banner of 'us'!"

Her strike hit directly at the paradox of the 'us' frame that James Mercer had agonized over. The heat and humidity of the basement tangled together, making it hard to breathe.

Maya Preston dropped her pen onto the table. It landed on the solid, organized sentences she had drafted.

"Enough with the emotional posturing."

Her voice, cold as ice, silenced everyone.

"There are only two questions we need to answer. 'Do we act, or do we not?' And if we do, 'How?' If we cannot agree on the first, this meeting is over, and we should all disperse."

Her cold summary brought the chill back to the basement air.

Raven pointed back at the monitor.

"The core data port inside Atlas Dynamics has opened. It is a temporary protocol that can disable the safety lock on the Androids. If we don't decide now, this opportunity will vanish forever."

The blue light of the core analysis diagram on the screen reflected coldly on the faces of Kevin and Karen.

---

Kevin’s patience finally snapped.

"Give me an alternative, Chairwoman!"

*BANG!*

Kevin’s fist slammed into the steel table. A threatening, percussive sound that seemed to make the entire basement tremble.

"How much longer do you intend to live as their plaything within the boundaries of what they call 'legal'?"

Karen rose slowly. Her chair pushed back completely. She stared into Kevin’s eyes, biting her lip.

"I..."

She seemed at a loss for words, gasping for air. The tension was so thick it felt as though she might bolt from the basement, but her clenched fingertips were trembling. She, too, was being shredded between the necessity of acting against this absurd world and the moral boundary of not becoming a monster.

She stood there, unable to take a step.

---

## Episode 003: The Decision

"Why don't we change the question?"

Maya Preston broke the silence in a calm voice. Everyone’s gaze converged on her sharp eyes.

"We have been trapped by the wrong question: 'Why should we commit terrorism?' If we presuppose the means—'terrorism'—and then look for a pretext, we inevitably fall into the contradiction of becoming the monsters we hate."

She closed her folder and looked between Kevin and Karen.

"The real question we must ask is this: **'Who are we fighting for?'**"

Kevin’s pupils wavered. He realized the massive shift in the frame that this change in questioning brought.

"We fight for the ordinary citizens and workers being sacrificed by the machine system created by greedy capitalists. If so, where should our muzzles and blades be pointed?"

Kevin walked toward the map with long strides. His voice now carried a clear, different kind of conviction.

"Maya is right. The target is wrong. Our enemy is not the innocent public on the street, but the corrupt leaders who design this merciless system and lick the cream off the top."

He pointed to the red marks on the map.

"CEO Kevin Dunn of NY Medical Center, who cuts workers to squeeze profits; Hospital CEO Steven Hart, who introduced robots without limits to crush rival hospitals; and Atlas Dynamics CEO Nathan Cole, who covered up defects and deceived the world with the frame of 'National Competitiveness.' They are our true targets."

He turned to face Karen.

"We are not terrorists attacking innocent citizens. We are judges attempting to condemn the cowardly, greedy system of leaders who betrayed the nation."

Karen chewed on Kevin’s words, slowly nodding. Her expression remained heavy and painful, but at least she had found a minimum sense of legitimacy.

---

"Fine," Karen said, sitting back down with determination.

"I agree with the shift in the question. But I have three non-negotiable conditions for joining this cause."

She held up three fingers.

"First, under no circumstances will we target innocent civilians. Not a single drop of innocent blood must be spilled."

"Second, our actions must not be simple physical assassinations, but a clear 'means of message delivery' to expose their sins to the public. Death itself must not be the goal."

"Third... at the end of this path of blood, we will never become monsters like them. The moment we cross that line, I will destroy this organization with my own hands."

Kevin stared straight into Karen’s eyes.

"Accepted. Those three conditions will be the absolute code of conduct for our organization."

Kevin looked around at the others. Henry Nash nodded deeply, and Raven and Gear expressed their consent with solemn silence.

The decision had passed.

---

The moment the decision was made, the basement began to function like a set of organic gears.

Raven ran to the communications nook to begin hacking the personal movements and security protocols of the targets. Gear went into the workshop to begin wiring the ominous mechanical device that was still missing its outer casing. Sparks from the electric welder periodically tore through the basement darkness.

Henry Nash approached Kevin and handed him the primary target plan written by Maya Preston.

Kevin stared at the faces of the three people printed on the paper. Without hesitation, he picked up one photograph with his finger.

A fleeting silence as he laid the picture on the metal table.

*Tap.*

"We start here."

Kevin’s fingertip pressed down on the forehead of the person in the photo. His hand was trembling ever so slightly, but none in the basement noticed. It was a mixture of fear for the impending prelude of blood and the final, human scream before heading toward an irreversible ruin.

---

At the same time, the staff breakroom at NY Medical Center.

Patricia Wells sat in a daze, staring at her smartphone screen during her night shift. On the news channels, the violent backlash from the labor sector following the congressional vote was being aired, along with a menacing 'declaration of war' sent by an unidentified anti-expansion organization.

`[BREAKING: Radical Anti-Expansion group warns of terrorism: "We will condemn those who designed the system of greed."]`

"Really... is a real terrorist attack actually happening?"

Patricia hugged herself as goosebumps washed over her. It was terrifying and trembling work. But the strange emotion blooming in a corner of her heart was not just fear.

'Why is it that I find the warnings of those lunatics... terrifying, yet somehow understandable?'

The cold sense of alienation hidden behind the sweetness of UBI, and the bizarre eyes of Mia, the machine that had been smiling gently without a soul by the patient's side...

Patricia shook her hand as she opened the search bar on her smartphone again. This time, without hesitation, she pressed down on the keys.

`robot facial expression weird cn04`

The search term blinked in blue on the dark liquid crystal screen. The moment she pressed Enter, a cold tension brushed against Patricia's fingertips.

Like the blue monitor light in the basement, a new wave of awakening was quietly beginning.
