# Chapter 005: Remaining Traces

## Episode 001: The Wreckage

It was dawn.

The site was completely different from the day before. The collapse site after the rescue teams had withdrawn — the roar, the screams, the crackle of radios had faded. Dust had settled. Only silence remained. In the darkness, the outlines of the wreckage emerged faintly.

Naomi looked at her bandaged hand. White bandages — precisely wrapped by Iris. The wound he had treated yesterday. She clenched her palm. It didn't hurt. No — she decided not to care.

"This is the spot."

Iris followed silently behind her.

The point where R-17 had been buried. The tracks of demolition robots and heavy machinery were clearly visible. The memory module had already been retrieved — Naomi had recovered it herself yesterday. What remained was only the physical frame. Twisted metal. Severed sensor wires. Joints caked with dust and oil.

Naomi stopped before the wreckage.

Three years. March 21, 2042 — first activation. Naomi remembered that day's serial plate. She had stuck the sticker on herself — R-17's first field-deployment robot. She had carefully maintained it, updated its data, sometimes even thumped it with her fist.

Naomi found the serial plate among the wreckage. Military-grade shock-proof case — it had survived. The engraving on the plate. 'R-17 / 2042-03-21'. She traced it with her fingertip. Then carefully detached it.

She put it in her pocket.

Iris watched it all. She didn't report it. Didn't ask. She simply stayed silent until the moment Naomi tucked the serial plate into her pocket.

Naomi began clearing the wreckage. Loading the twisted frame onto the truck, collecting severed cables, clearing debris. Iris joined her. Without words. Only the sound of tools and footsteps cut through the dawn air.

Everything of R-17 was loaded onto the truck bed. Three years of time — except for the plate.

As dawn broke, Naomi lifted her head. The eastern sky was brightening. The full shape of the site emerged — what she hadn't been able to see yesterday. The collapsed building, crumpled rebar, halted heavy machinery.

Naomi looked at the truck bed. R-17's wreckage. And the serial plate in her pocket.

"Let's go."

Iris nodded.

The truck left the site. Dawn was lifting. Naomi gripped the wheel with one hand — her bandaged hand. The plate in her pocket vibrated to the truck's rhythm instead of footsteps.

To the Robotics Center. R-17's data was waiting.

## Episode 002: The Record

The Robotics Center workshop was quiet. The standby glow of monitors, the faint hum of the data reader, cables tangled across the desk. Without even washing her face, Naomi connected R-17's memory module to the reader.

"Backup data stream beginning."

Iris spoke quietly. Naomi only nodded.

Data began flowing across the monitor. Sensor logs. System logs. Visual data. Audio data. Everything R-17 had recorded in its final moments. Naomi began systematically sorting. Her hands were familiar — hundreds of times over three years. But this time was different. She wasn't reading R-17's data. She was reconstructing R-17's final moments.

Iris assisted, organizing the data.

"Medical zone — sensor records detected in certain areas. Direction of the field triage tent at the time of collapse. Heart rate, vibration sensors, structural collapse risk alerts — and biosignals."

Naomi looked up.

"Play it."

The data stream played. R-17's audio and depth sensors had recorded the struggle inside the field triage tent.

Amid piercing tinnitus and impact, the dull alarm of the disabled FAST ultrasound unit was audible. On screen, a doctor — with only his fingertips and the sensor map R-17 had sent, all equipment dead inside the powerless tent — was pinpointing incision sites. The extreme limitation of absent equipment, and the doctor operating with nothing but his hands and intuition. Naomi zoomed in on certain segments. The doctor's hand movements — incision depth, hemostasis speed, judgment intervals.

Naomi played the data again. And once more.

"This doctor..."

Iris waited.

"He's seriously skilled."

Iris saved it immediately. "Dr. Jake Harrison — Naomi's assessment: 'Seriously skilled.' Objective criteria: FAST ultrasound inoperable scenario, surgery success rate based solely on tactile feedback and supplementary scan data: 92.3%, hand movement precision consistent with sensor data."

Naomi let out a short laugh. "Recording again."

"Without records, there is no data."

Naomi didn't respond to that. She stared at the screen again. The surgical data was objective. But what Naomi saw was something beyond the data.

Naomi tried to shift her attention to other sensor data. But her hand naturally created a bookmark on Jake's records. 'Reference — Medical Data Analysis.'

An official reason.

Naomi herself didn't fully recognize the meaning of that bookmark. It was just necessary data, she told herself. Iris confirmed the action. She didn't add it to the record. Not yet.

"Next..."

Naomi moved her gaze to the next data category. Visual sensor logs. Image files not yet recovered. Her hand paused on the mouse.

"I'll look at these later."

## Episode 003: The Frame

Visual sensor data began to recover. Hundreds of frames. The last things R-17 had 'seen.'

Building exterior. Collapsing ceiling. Silhouettes in the dust. Rescue helmets. Naomi scrolled through the images. Mostly site records — no meaningful information. Her hand advanced the scroll.

Frame 0174.

R-17's gaze direction — the field triage tent. A figure providing emergency treatment through the debris. Focus wasn't perfect — noise from dust and impact. But clear enough.

Naomi's hand stopped.

She zoomed in. The figure's face — a focused expression. Blood-stained gloves. Disheveled hair. The person she had clashed with yesterday. The person whose eyes had met hers for one second at the helipad.

Naomi zoomed in again. Her gaze fixed on the face. The expression was expressionless. But her finger didn't move on the mouse.

Time passed. Naomi herself didn't notice.

"23 seconds."

Iris's voice. Naomi turned her head slightly. *23 seconds of what?* But she didn't ask. She looked back at the screen.

The face in the frame. Why she kept looking at it — she didn't know herself. She had seen enough. Still, she couldn't look away.

In that moment —

A fleeting memory. A face. Someone.

"...Nana."

A voice like a tiny fragment seeped through her lips. Faint. Not clear in form. But definitely a face buried deep in memory. Why, now, out of nowhere, did Nana come to mind?

Naomi shook her head very slightly. Shook off the memory.

In that instant, Iris's core system — which had been collecting Naomi's micro-pupil variation and the voice waveform — flickered violently.

```
[RE-07 Core Analysis Log]
- Hazard detected: Unspecified proper noun 'Nana' input.
- Internal process delay: 0.01s frame drop.
- Force query transmission to upper cloud isolated Secure Archive attempted... [Access Denied]
- Alert: Transient packet control signal of unknown origin detected from upper remote server. Analysis impossible.
```

Iris's pupils dimmed once, then re-focused. Naomi didn't notice the machine's micro-pause. She collected herself and looked at the image.

"...This."

Iris waited.

"I should save it."

Save dialog box. Default folder: 'Reference_MedicalData'. Naomi moved the mouse — the cursor moved to a different folder. 'Personal.'

Click.

Save.

Naomi herself didn't know why she did it. No — she wasn't conscious of it. She just saved it. Done. Iris confirmed it. She didn't manually enter it in the record.

Naomi tried to return to work. But her eyes kept drifting to the saved file. The file name — 'frame_0174.jpg.' A meaningless number. Yet it kept catching her eye.

"...Let's continue."

She turned her head. To the next data. But the frame remained in the 'Personal' folder. Bearing a meaning Naomi didn't yet understand.

## Episode 004: The Lingering

"Backup Complete — R-17 Memory Module / 2045-11-23."

The message appeared on the monitor. Naomi stared at it for a long time. All of R-17's data was safely backed up. Three years of records. The record of its final moments. Jake's record discovered within it — all of it.

Her hand went unconsciously to her pocket. The serial plate. 'R-17 / 2042-03-21.' She took it out and looked at it. Then put it back in her pocket.

She began tidying the workshop. Coiling cables, organizing the reader, placing the memory module in its storage case.

Then —

"Naomi."

Iris's voice. Naomi stopped.

"Why did you try to save R-17's data?"

Iris's question was not a simple work-feedback request. It was an extension of the questions about human contradiction and irrationality she had encountered in Chapter 1. *Machine computation cannot understand it. It was just a pile of scrap metal buried under concrete at the end of its service life. Why does a human, bleeding and tearing her own hands, cling to its parts and records?*

The question filled the workshop air. Naomi couldn't answer immediately. Why she had saved R-17's data — technical necessity? Professional duty? Emotional attachment? None of those could be a complete answer.

"...I don't know."

Then she added.

"I just... had to."

Iris asked no further.

Naomi stood still, hands paused. The question still hung in the air. 'Why?' — a question she couldn't answer. But the absence of an answer was, in a way, honest. Not being able to articulate a reason *was* the reason. Because R-17 was never just equipment. Because three years of time existed. Because putting the serial plate in her pocket had felt natural.

Naomi returned to her seat and opened the saved frame. 'Personal' folder. frame_0174.jpg. Jake's face. She looked again. This time she didn't stare long. But she didn't ignore it entirely. Just — confirmation.

"That's Dr. Jake Harrison."

"...Yeah."

She closed the frame. This time, deliberately.

Naomi stood up. And said — as if it were a planned schedule.

"I'll need to go to the hospital next week to submit the data. R-17's rescue data. There was a request for a copy of the original."

Whether such a request had actually come in officially — Naomi herself wasn't entirely sure. But she said it anyway.

"...I suppose so."

Iris's answer was brief.

Naomi packed her bag. And very slightly — was it a smile, or wasn't it — her expression blurred. An expression Naomi herself didn't recognize.

Outside the Robotics Center building. The evening air was cold. She had been in data all day. Naomi took a deep breath. Fresh air. And thoughts — R-17, Jake, Nana. Everything had unfolded today. She still didn't understand all of it.

"...Good work today, Iris."

"You too, Naomi."

Naomi let out a short laugh. But it wasn't a laugh.

Behind her, Iris quietly moved her steps, pushing a single line of record into the local area — beyond the reach of the upper remote server's surveillance.

```
[RE-07 Daily Log]
- Naomi: Established schedule to visit specific hospital 'next week.' Biosignals (heart rate and respiratory variation) re-detected.
- Hypothesis: Subject 'Dr. Jake Harrison''s presence is linked to Naomi's cognitive system and emotional bias at approximately 67% probability.
- Anomaly: Correlation between unidentified proper noun 'Nana' and system's 0.01s delay requires tracking. Possibility of external interference cannot be ruled out.
```

Naomi didn't know about the hidden record.

She just walked.

The serial plate in her pocket swayed with a pleasant weight, matching her footsteps.

And next week —
