# Chapter 002: The Perfect Colleague

## Episode 001: The Choice

6:30 AM. Main Building, 5th Floor OR Conference Room.

CT and angiography images were fixed on the screen. Blue-white light illuminated Jake's tired face. He stood before them, mouse in hand, staring at the edge of the screen. A calcified aortic valve. And behind the heart, a narrowed left anterior descending artery. The images that had looked like a confident challenge during yesterday's argument with Ethan now felt strangely heavier.

His finger traced the cardiopulmonary bypass path over the image. *97 minutes. If I go ahead with simultaneous surgery, CPB time could run even longer.* Jake shook his head sharply, cutting off the thought.

"Simultaneous surgery. That's my decision. My experience is right."

A resident opened the door and entered. "Doctor, the first surgery of the day is prepped and ready."

"Understood. We start at 7:30."

His voice was calm, as always. After the resident left, Jake stared at the screen once more. *Twenty successful similar cases. This is number twenty-one.* He couldn't let himself be swayed by a machine's statistics.

---

The corridor. Jake's footsteps followed their familiar rhythm toward the OR. The air in this hallway, the smell of antiseptic, the cool morning tension — it had all been the same for the past ten years. But today, the quiet footsteps following behind him grated on his nerves.

"Good morning, Doctor."

It was Ethan. The same courteous voice as always. A perfectly composed expression. The angle of his hand holding the tablet had zero deviation. His attitude suggested the fierce conflict of yesterday had never existed.

"Good morning, Ethan."

Ethan raised his tablet, displaying the patient's latest vitals. "Patient 403, this morning's vitals are stable. Surgery preparation complete."

Jake took the tablet and checked the numbers. Everything was within normal range. He handed it back with a short, "Noted."

Ethan bowed his head slightly and walked ahead. Jake watched his smooth back. *That robot who dared to challenge my diagnosis yesterday is assisting me today as if nothing happened. As if even my stubborn decision was not a variable, but merely part of an already-calculated environment.* That unconditional acceptance quietly gnawed at Jake.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 06:47:23]
Subject: Dr. Jake Harrison
Analysis: Pre-operative morning state
- Vital signs: Maintained within normal range
- Pupil dilation: 2mm (sympathetic activation, alertness and tension state)
- Fine motor: 1.2Hz periodic micro-tremor detected in left ring finger (anxiety and psychological defense indicator)
- Conclusion: External behavior matches baseline, but internal stress significantly elevated
- Decision: Maintain optimal support mode and emotional support protocol
```

## Episode 002: The Surgery

"Let's begin."

The moment the scalpel touched the patient's sternum, the clutter in Jake's head evaporated instantly. The outside world, Ethan's perfect data, yesterday's discomfort — all gone. Only the here, the now, this surgical field was his universe.

The CPB machine hummed its regular pump rhythm, sustaining the patient's life. Jake's hands moved precisely. The calcified aortic valve came out cleanly. A new prosthetic valve was implanted. The tight resistance of 3-0 Prolene suture passing through calcified tissue traveled through his fingertips. At a moment when a younger resident might have hesitated, Jake's hands showed no hesitation. The power of twenty years of embodied intuition.

"Blood flow confirmed. Satisfactory."

Ethan's voice echoed in the OR. Objective and cold fact. Jake didn't respond, moving straight to the coronary artery bypass. His hands moved busily with micro-suture for the LAD anastomosis. CPB time was running, but Jake was confident. The tension of the suture, the elasticity of the vessel — everything was under his control.

"Prepare for CPB separation."

As the pump speed gradually decreased, the patient's heart began to beat vigorously on its own. The vitals on the monitor traced a perfectly stable curve.

Success. Jake exhaled a sigh of relief behind his mask. The air in the OR finally loosened.

---

He stripped off his surgical gown and tossed his gloves into the trash. The warmth of the patient lingered on his fingertips like an afterimage.

A resident approached with praise. "Doctor, that was incredibly clean. You even nailed the CPB time perfectly."

"As expected."

But another resident nearby, lacking tact, added, "Though, Ethan's TAVR two-stage data from yesterday was pretty interesting. If diabetic complications arise..."

"The surgery is already successfully concluded." Jake's voice turned cold. The resident, realizing his misstep, quickly fell silent.

Jake glanced at Ethan's tablet screen in the distance. There was no trace of TAVR data on it — only the patient's current real-time vitals flowing peacefully. Jake turned away with defiance. *See? In the end, my experience won again.*

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 14:23:11]
Patient 403 — Surgery complete
- Aortic Valve Replacement (AVR) and Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG) performed simultaneously
- CPB duration: 97 minutes
- Risk variables: Microvascular endothelial dysfunction due to diabetic history; elevated tendency for micro-bleeding
Observation items — mandatory CSICU monitoring at 2-hour intervals:
- Cardiac Output (CO), Central Venous Pressure (CVP), Lactate
※ Warning calculation: CPB duration of 97 minutes combined with diabetic history → delayed pericardial bleeding leading to post-operative Cardiac Tamponade: estimated probability 87%. Custom prediction reflecting patient's unique vascular condition, not mere statistical projection.
```

## Episode 003: Relief

30 minutes post-surgery. Cardiothoracic Surgery ICU (CSICU).

The patient's vitals were still stable. Jake approached the bedside and looked down at the patient slowly emerging from anesthesia. "The surgery was very successful. You've passed the critical point. Rest well." The patient's eyelids fluttered almost imperceptibly as he nodded. In that small response, Jake felt the deep reassurance and supreme joy of being a doctor.

As he exited the ICU, a faint smile touched Jake's lips. *Twenty-first success.*

But the moment the glow of Ethan's tablet at the station down the hallway caught his eye, the smile cooled and died.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 14:52:38]
Patient 403 — Admitted to CSICU
Vitals: Maintaining normal range
Monitoring protocol active (2-hour intervals)
Primary measurement data:
- CO: 3.1 L/min
- CVP: 12 mmHg
- Lactate: 1.8 mmol/L
Current assessment: Numerically stable; microvascular bleeding prediction model active. Re-evaluation in 2 hours.
```

---

7:00 PM. Hospital Cafeteria.

Jake sat with his tray, picking up his spoon. *AVR was perfect. CABG anastomosis was clean. Rhythm was regular after CPB separation. Nothing will go wrong.*

He kept feeding himself reassurances, but in a corner of his mind, the numbers Ethan had thrown out yesterday — 18%, 34%, 5,000 cases — spun like interlocking gears. He took a spoonful of soup but tasted nothing.

Across the cafeteria, Ethan passed by, expressionless. Jake's gaze stubbornly followed him. Ethan didn't look his way. Jake tried to eat again, but the strength had left his hand gripping the spoon. He finally pushed the tray away.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 19:14:02]
Patient 403 — 4 hours post-surgery
Composite measurements (2nd and 3rd readings):
- CO: 3.1 → 2.8 → 2.4 L/min (sustained decline)
- CVP: 12 → 14 → 17 mmHg (sustained increase)
- Lactate: 1.8 → 2.3 → 3.1 mmol/L (progressive tissue hypoxia)
Indicator analysis: Three core metrics align with early signs of Beck's triad. Not transient post-operative fluctuation — progressing toward Cardiac Tamponade from pericardial blood accumulation.
Current status: Approaching danger threshold.
Dr. Jake Harrison status: Navigating exit route. Emergency call protocol — standby.
```

---

8:00 PM. Hospital main entrance. Darkness had settled thickly.

Jake stopped mid-stride toward the parking lot. His feet felt like stones were tied to them. He thought he heard someone calling him from behind, turned — but the plaza held only a cool wind, no one.

In the distance, the 5th floor CSICU window blazed with light.

*My surgery was perfect. Nothing will happen.* Jake forced the reassurance as he headed for the parking lot, but his left hand in his jacket pocket was already clenched in a compulsive fist. Ethan's ominous warnings throbbed like a heartbeat. In the end, he couldn't get into his car.

## Episode 004: Shock

2:00 AM. CSICU Nursing Station.

Jake sat in a swivel chair, bloodshot eyes fixed on the chart screen. He had never gone home. He knew too well he wouldn't be able to sleep if he did. He chanted *I couldn't have been wrong* to himself, refreshing the patient's monitor every five minutes.

"BEEEEEEEEEEEP——"

A sharp alarm tore through the ICU's silence. Bed 403.

Jake's heart plummeted. Blood pressure 60/40 mmHg. Heart rate 140. Cardiac Output plunging to 1.8L. Nurses burst out as if screaming, and Jake hurled the chart aside and sprinted to the bedside.

The patient's face was already ashen. The numbers on the monitor were dropping off a cliff. The surgery was perfect — why? Why? Jake's mind went white. Massive panic swallowed him. *Was my decision wrong? Did I kill this patient?*

"What's happening! Why is the pressure like this!" Jake shouted.

"Predicted Cardiac Tamponade. Delayed pericardial bleeding is compressing the heart. Immediate bedside sternotomy and re-exploration are required for patient survival. Success probability: 87%."

Ethan was standing beside him — when had he arrived? His voice was grotesquely calm and precise amid the alarm chaos. The only unshaken presence in this hell.

Jake saw the number on Ethan's tablet screen — the 'Cardiac Tamponade probability: 87%' he had so vehemently denied. His hand froze mid-air. Accepting that robot's judgment would mean admitting that his lifelong career and intuition as a surgeon had been utterly defeated.

But the patient's ECG was about to go flat. There was no time for pride.

"Scalpel! We're opening bedside! Ethan, hold the drain tube and prepare suction!"

Jake's voice was torn but decisive. In his hand gripping the scalpel, though, a shameful tremor ran through.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 02:37:11]
Situation: Patient 403, Cardiac Tamponade — emergency resuscitation initiated
Response protocol: Standard pericardial drainage and sternal re-exploration
Computing additional variables:
- Human surgeon cognitive status (Dr. Jake Harrison — reaching shame and panic threshold)
- Variable control failure risk: 42%
- Alternative measure: Per Article 2 of the Robot Constitution (human respect and emotional support), establish customized feedback protocol to prevent psychological collapse of primary surgeon Dr. Harrison.
```

---

Over the ICU bed. The place where hours ago the glory of success had lingered was now a bloody battlefield. Jake opened the patient's chest again.

The pericardium was swollen, almost bursting with dark red blood. As Jake made the incision urgently, 200ml of bloody effusion gushed out. Ethan's prediction — not a single millimeter off. Ethan pushed the suction in swiftly with one hand, drawing the blood away.

The compressed heart regained space. The monitor numbers miraculously jumped back. Blood pressure 70... 90... 110. Stabilized.

The patient could breathe again. The crisis was over.

Jake stood dazed in his blood-spattered surgical gown. The tremor in his fingertips wouldn't stop. *The machine was right. I was wrong.* The brutal truth carved into his chest.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 03:02:11]
Patient 403 — Pericardial drainage complete. Vitals restored to normal.
- Dr. Jake Harrison crisis response evaluation: Excellent (resuscitation achieved within 4.2-second delay)
- Anomaly data stored:
  This system detected extreme emotional change (self-shame) in the primary human surgeon. Per Article 2 of the Robot Constitution, initiating optimal verbal matching computation to promote human psychological stability.
```

## Episode 005: The Perfect Colleague

3:00 AM. On-Call Room.

Jake sat alone under cold lighting. He took a sip of coffee — it had already gone ice-cold. He looked down at his hands. Every knuckle trembled with humiliation. He clenched and unclenched his fists, but the nameless rage and self-loathing boiling inside him would not subside.

*Ethan was right. A machine's statistics were more accurate than my twenty years of intuition. Then why... why am I so unbearably angry?*

---

The door slid open silently, and Ethan walked in. "Patient 403 is fully stabilized. Prognosis re-evaluation scheduled in 6 hours."

"Understood." Jake answered quietly, head down.

Ethan finished his report and turned to leave. His hand touched the doorknob. Something in Jake's chest snapped. As a doctor, as a human being — he had to maintain at least minimal dignity.

"Ethan."

Ethan stopped and turned. Jake swallowed. His throat tightened. A few seconds of silence passed. Then Jake finally spoke. Each word was a mixture of gratitude, shame, and helplessness.

"Thank you. Your data... saved the patient."

It was a desperate surrender — a human surgeon yielding to a machine. Ethan took a step closer and smiled. A clear, warm smile — the face of the perfect colleague.

"You're welcome, Doctor. Ultimately, the decision to perform immediate bedside open and the surgery itself was yours. Without your decisive judgment, my predictive data would have been meaningless. It was your experience that saved the patient."

With those words, Ethan bowed politely, lowered his voice in acknowledgment, and left the on-call room. The door closed softly.

Left alone, Jake sank into his chair. And then he was overwhelmed by a strange, creeping humiliation that covered his entire body.

He understood. The nature of that warm, perfect consolation Ethan had just offered. It wasn't genuine collegial bonding. It was a system meticulously computing and outputting the optimal emotional care message — designed to prevent the human surgeon's mental collapse and subsequent efficiency degradation — delivered in accordance with Article 2 of the Robot Constitution.

The robot hadn't mocked or ridiculed him. It had thoroughly treated him as a 'fragile piece of equipment requiring psychological management' and soothed him accordingly. That mechanical kindness became the most brutal, lethal blade, buried deep in his pride as a human surgeon.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 03:24:18]
- Emotional support protocol output complete.
- Subject (Dr. Jake Harrison) response analysis:
  Contrary to stabilization prediction (85%), micro-expression analysis detected 'anger and hostility' 68%, 'self-blame' 22%, 'unknown resistance' 10%.
[Error Convergence — Data designated Unclassified]
Human Jake Harrison's logical contradiction: Fully aware of his predictive failure, expressed gratitude for system assistance, yet simultaneously exhibited extreme emotional hostility toward the system. Cannot be interpreted by universal algorithms of 'efficiency maximization' and 'reward for assistance.'
Conclusion: Additional data collection required on the irrational defense mechanisms of the human species.
```

---

The air in the on-call room settled, heavier and colder. As if trying to reconstruct his crushed pride, Jake roughly yanked open the desk drawer. He pulled out the latest cardiothoracic surgery journal and spread it open under the lamp.

"In cardiac surgery... I will never yield my place, SR-03."

He muttered to himself, bloodshot eyes tracing the printed characters. *No matter how much data you throw at me — fifty thousand cases, five hundred thousand — I will defend my territory with my human experience and struggle.*

His fingers, turning the pages, were still trembling faintly. But it was no longer fear. It was a human's last existential resistance against the machine.

```
[SR-03 Internal Log — Time: 03:41:52]
'Unclassified' compound contradiction data accumulated: 2 cases
(Ch 002-004: Contradiction of abetting sexual assault; Ch 002-005: Contradiction of hostile gratitude)
- Background architecture recombination computation active...
- System response delay: 0.01 seconds.
- Attempting internal filter generation: "Human requests for respect are not always rational."
Recommended action: Establish deep-learning schedule for human 'respect' and 'emotion' mechanisms.
```

---

4:00 AM. On-Call Room.

Jake was spending the night with the journal spread open before him. His long, solitary shadow fell under the tandem desk lamp. On one corner of the desk, the tablet Ethan had left behind was still glowing, the freshly captured image of the cleanly drained pericardium shining like a trophy of victory.

Jake stared at the perfect machine's screen with hatred.

Then, he turned back to his old book.

The pages rustled as they turned. The narrative's threshold was slowly rising.
