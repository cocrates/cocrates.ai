# Chapter 004: Accident Site

## Episode 001: Collapse

A roar.

Coffee splashed from Naomi's hand. The monitor on her desk shook. The sound of something breaking — the ground trembled.

Her pager went off.

— *Rescue robot R-17, buried at building collapse site. Immediate deployment.*

R-17.

Naomi was already on her feet. She shut down the monitor, strapped on her tool belt. Her hands remembered — the serial plate from its first activation three years ago, the last line of its maintenance log, the sensor values of the left joint she had replaced last week.

"RE-07."

Iris approached. Her eyes were different — no longer in static analysis mode, but carrying a prepared movement.

"Switching to rescue terrain analysis mode. Requesting accompaniment."

Naomi nodded. No words were needed. Weeks of accumulated trust. In training, in daily routines — a silent rhythm they had built together.

They ran together.

---

The site was hell.

Dust covered the sky. Debris scattered across the road. Screams, radios, sirens — every sound mixed together, piercing the ears.

Static crackled from the radio. A rescue worker's voice cut in and out. The communications network was unstable.

"A hardline data link is required." Iris's voice was calm. "Wireless reliability is 62%. We need a wired connection to access R-17's memory."

Naomi nodded, already walking. Debris underfoot — glass shards, rebar, concrete dust. And through it all, R-17's signal.

"There."

The burial point. One face of the building had completely collapsed. Beneath it, R-17's upper body was pinned under a concrete slab. Only one arm jutted out.

Iris stood at the edge of the debris, beginning a scan. Her pupils glowed faintly. "Analyzing entry path. R-17 burial point approach priority. Stability 73%. Path secured — 15 minutes."

Then —

"What do you think you're doing?!"

A voice tore through the air behind her. Naomi turned. A man in scrubs with a protective gown over them — his face red with exhaustion and anger.

"Are you a rescue worker? A robot technician?"

Naomi didn't stop her hands. "I need this robot's data."

"People are dying! Right now, this instant!"

Their eyes met. First encounter. Dust from the site cut a path between them.

"Without that robot's data," Naomi said. Her voice was low. "You can't save anyone either."

Jake's lips parted, then closed. He tried to say something, but nothing came out. Instead, he turned. Began walking toward the medical tent.

Iris watched the exchange. A line was added to her internal log.

— Human identity matching complete: Dr. Jake Harrison. Cardiothoracic surgery. Physician responsible for SR-03. Advocates human rescue-first principle. Current assessment: Caution.

Jake walked. His pace was quick and firm. But his hand unconsciously brushed his waist — where Ethan's portable ultrasound usually hung. Empty.

At the hospital, Ethan's perfect data had felt suffocating and oppressive. But now, thrown into this hell of dust and screams without any equipment, the machine's absence cut paradoxically to the bone. *If Ethan were here... he could have pinpointed internal bleeding points in 0.1 seconds with 3D mapping, classified survivor signals in the rubble.*

He pulled his hand away. His fingers brushed his glove and stopped.

*I have to do it.*

He put on surgical gloves. His hands didn't shake.

---

"Naomi. Your hand."

Iris's voice brought her back to reality. Naomi looked down. Her palm — embedded with debris. Red liquid was dripping down the tool handle.

"I'm fine. Don't worry about it."

"Naomi Hayes — pain threshold exceeded. Continuing R-17 memory access. Observation: injury disregard pattern."

Naomi connected the data cable. The memory module was deeper inside. She reached in. Debris cut into her flesh, but her hand didn't waver.

"Additional access — possible. Risk increasing." Iris's voice. "Probability: 62%."

"Do it. I can do it."

The data line glowed. A hand reaching for the memory module. Blood, dust, and the line.

---

## Episode 002: Each

Field triage tent. Canvas flapped in the wind. Rescue teams kept bringing in casualties.

Jake was sorting the seriously injured. Feeling compression sites with his hands, listening to breath sounds with his stethoscope.

Even the FAST ultrasound was inoperable from the dust and impact. The portable X-ray battery was already dead. Only the tactile sensitivity of his fingertips, his stethoscope, and humanity's imperfect experience remained.

"This patient — given the posture and compression site, internal bleeding is suspected. But I can't pinpoint the exact location."

He set down the stethoscope. His hand moved unconsciously — toward his waist. Empty space. Ethan's ultrasound usually hung there. Gone.

Jake pulled his hand back. His fingers brushed his glove and stopped.

The patient's condition was deteriorating. The monitor's alarm quickened.

"I need to locate the bleeding. Right now."

*If Ethan were here...*

He didn't finish the thought. The overwhelming helplessness in the absence of the machine constricted his whole body. *Without being able to determine the extent of crush injuries, using a scalpel here is a gamble.*

At that moment, the sharp voice of the engineer he had just confronted echoed in his ears.

— *"Without that robot's data, you can't save anyone either."*

Jake clamped his mouth shut as if to grind his teeth, shaking off the thought.

---

Same time. Naomi was pushing her body into a narrow gap in the rubble. The concrete edge scraped her shoulder. Blood ran from her hand, but she paid no attention.

"Heart rate elevated. Blood pressure normal range. Stress response detected." Iris's voice came through the earpiece. "Pain response disregard pattern reconfirmed."

Naomi almost told her to be quiet — but held back.

The memory module was visible. Wedged between debris. Firmly lodged.

"I'm in. But extraction will take more time."

"Additional collapse risk — 38%. Rising per hour."

"Enough. I can do it."

Naomi pulled out her tools. The wound on her palm left red marks on the tool handle.

"Please. Please be alive."

The words weren't spoken aloud — they seeped through her lips.

---

Triage tent entrance.

Iris stood there. Data pad in hand. Information confirmed through the rescue robots' communication network — victim positions, postures, debris distribution. It was segmented from medical data, but could serve as supplementary information for medical judgment.

"The medical team needs this data."

Iris walked into the tent. She faced Jake. Second encounter.

"Rescue data. Victim positions and posture data. Supplementary information for medical judgment."

Jake accepted the data pad. 0.3 seconds of static. Confusion.

But the moment he checked the data, his expression changed.

"How... was this data collected?"

"Based on RE-07's omnidirectional sensors and R-17's pre-scan data. Accuracy: 94.2%."

Jake hesitated. The arrogance that had distrusted and resisted machine data — broken before the absolute truth of a patient's death. He accepted the data.

"...Thank you."

Iris recorded that word.

— Jake Harrison. 'Thank you' utterance recorded. SR-03 supervising physician. Data point registered.

Jake's expression changed. He re-evaluated the patient's condition based on the data. Possibility emerged.

---

## Episode 003: The Data

Inside the rubble gap. Narrow and dark. A single flashlight illuminated the space.

Naomi's hand — debris-embedded palm connected the data cable. Blood smeared. But her hand didn't shake.

"I've got contact. The memory module — it's alive."

"Heart rate elevated. Hormonal change — joy? Relief?"

"Unclassified."

The last sentence echoed only inside Iris.

The memory module was detached. Naomi carefully extracted it. Connected it to the data reader. Data streams flowed across the screen — sensor records from the moment of the building collapse. Victim positions, postures, compression range, data changes over time. Everything was alive.

"It's all recorded here. Every piece of data from the rescue."

"Data integrity: 97.8%. Recovery successful."

Naomi picked up the data pad. She had to go to Jake.

"Transmit to Dr. Harrison?"

"I need to deliver it in person."

---

Triage tent.

Jake was checking the condition of a critical patient. Evacuation prep — but the patient's condition was worsening. There was no time.

Then Naomi appeared. Data pad in hand.

She held it out without a word. Jake took it. Second encounter. A different atmosphere from the first — but still, no words.

R-17's sensor records unfolded on the data pad. 3D map. Victim positions and postures. Compression data.

Jake's expression changed.

"...This data..."

"That robot recorded it. At the time of the collapse."

Jake scrolled through the data. Victim posture, compression points, estimated damage range. Without ultrasound, without X-ray — this robot's data perfectly compensated for the incomplete diagnosis.

"...With this data, I can operate."

Naomi nodded. Jake nodded. Wordlessly.

A single phrase brushed through Jake's mind.

*...Saved. That robot's data saved them.*

It didn't leave his mouth. But the truth filled the air between them.

Jake revised the surgery plan based on the data. Naomi's words surfaced — *"Without that robot's data, you can't save anyone either."* She was right. Absolutely right.

---

Outside the triage tent. Iris watched the scene.

```
[RE-07 Internal Log — Time: 18:14:02]
- Jake Harrison — data acceptance time: 2.3s. Reduced by 1.2s from previous.
- Comparative analysis: Emotional resistance values during SR-03 data acceptance significantly decreased.
  Judged that the absolute necessity of data overwhelmed the human's pride threshold.

[Core analysis delay — 0.01s]
Cause: Computational overload from human irrational bonding and mutual recognition mechanisms.
(※ Alert: Same resonance frequency detected as frame drop phenomenon in SR-03 session logs.)
```

Naomi emerged from the tent. Jake was preparing for surgery inside. Iris approached Naomi. Naomi's hand — blood had dried.

"Naomi. Your hand. Treatment is needed."

---

## Episode 004: Cooperation

The surgery was a success. The victim was evacuated in stable condition. The site was moving into recovery phase.

Jake stepped outside the tent. Naomi was waiting. Organizing her data pad.

The two faced each other without words.

"...The data was helpful."

"Good."

Brief silence. Two people who had clashed stood in the same space.

Jake couldn't find more words. Neither could Naomi. They bowed their heads in acknowledgment and Jake returned to the tent. Naomi watched his back. What that brief exchange meant — neither spoke of it.

---

Site cleanup area.

Iris had brought a first aid kit. She began treating Naomi's hand. Precisely. Gently. As accurate as a medical robot.

"Biological damage treatment mode. Naomi, pain level?"

"I'm fine. Don't worry about it."

"Pain disregard pattern reconfirmed. Divergent from general human response. Recorded."

Naomi laughed. "You're always recording."

Iris paused briefly. "...Without records, there is no data. Without data, there is no learning. Without learning, I cannot understand humans."

Naomi stopped smiling. Her face turned serious.

"...Yeah. I suppose that's right."

A line in Iris's internal log.

— 'Yeah. I suppose that's right' — Agreement utterance. RE-07 data collection motive — human acceptance. Recorded.

Naomi's hand was bandaged. Clean and precise.

---

Jake stepped outside the tent again. To catch his breath. He reached for a cigarette — but there were none. He'd quit long ago.

His gaze went to the cleanup area. Naomi and Iris. A robot treating a human's hand. Their conversation was short, but seemed comfortable. The silence between their words felt natural.

Jake watched the scene.

*Ethan isn't comfortable like that beside me. Beside me, Ethan is always suffocatingly perfect and taut.*

The thought reached that point. And stopped.

As he turned away, Iris's gaze caught him.

"Dr. Jake Harrison. Observing us. For 7.3 seconds."

Naomi didn't look toward Jake. Instead, she asked Iris. "What do you think?"

"...Classifying."

"Can't classify it?"

"..."

Iris's silence.

Then, faintly carried on the wind — the distant sound of helicopter rotors. Not yet arrived. But soon.

Jake heard the sound and disappeared into the tent. Naomi looked in the direction of the sound.

---

The sun was setting. The dust at the site settled. Rescue teams began preparing to withdraw.

Iris organized today's data. Her pupils glowed faintly — data streams flowing within.

Her gaze swept the site. Rescue workers walked over the rubble. Dust scattered in the wind.

```
[RE-07 Internal Log — Time: 19:42:11]
- Human subject identity information mapping:
  * Subject A: Naomi Hayes (robot engineer, biosignal variation collection complete)
  * Subject B: Jake Harrison (cardiothoracic surgeon, SR-03 supervising physician)
- Human-human interaction analysis:
  Humans are identifying each other through 'afterimages' and 'emotional impact' without exchanging identity designations (names). Inefficient identification mechanism. This system has permanently mapped both subjects' identity codes to prevent data contamination.
```

In the distance, the helicopter's rotor sound grew closer. Not yet arrived. But unmistakably approaching.

---

## Episode 005: One Second

Helicopter rotors whipped up dust. Engine noise filled the site. Final victim evacuation preparations.

Naomi stood at the edge of the helipad, organizing her data pad. R-17's memory module safely stored. Jake was walking toward the helicopter. He had to return to the hospital, prepare for the next surgery.

The two faced each other, five meters apart.

Helicopter noise. Too loud for words.

One second.

Their eyes met.

Naomi's eyes — tired, but wanting to say something. A look of peculiar acknowledgment: *The arrogant doctor who shouted at a robot ended up saving a person with that machine's data.*

Jake's eyes — tired, but wanting to understand something. A look of desperate acceptance: *The crazy engineer who bled to dig out a robot's brain was right after all.*

No words passed between them. But one second was longer than words.

Naomi nodded. Jake nodded.

They turned back to their separate paths.

---

Fifty meters away. Iris observed the scene. Monitoring Naomi's biosignals through long-range sensors.

— Naomi — Heart rate: 78bpm. After encounter with Dr. Harrison: 83bpm. +5bpm increase. Respiratory rate variation detected.

— Interaction time: 1.2 seconds. Non-verbal exchange. Type: head nod. Content: Recognition? Acknowledgment? — Unclassified.

Data stored in memory. Analysis pending.

Iris began walking toward Naomi. The helicopter lifted off. Rotor wash stirred dust.

---

Naomi climbed into the vehicle. Iris was already inside.

"Naomi. Your hand. Pain?"

"Fine. Change the bandage when I get home."

"...Understood."

Naomi looked out the window. The helicopter was rising. Jake's helicopter.

Same time. Jake looked down from the helicopter. Naomi and Iris's vehicle. Growing smaller.

He thought about the day. Collision. Data. And one second. Jake remembered Naomi as 'the crazy engineer at the site,' and Naomi remembered Jake as 'the arrogant doctor.' Humans didn't even know each other's names.

But in Iris's database, their names and biometrics were chillingly accurate.

```
[RE-07 Daily Log Closed — Time: 20:15:00]
- Naomi Hayes: Biosignals entering stabilization phase. Within error range.
- Jake Harrison: New high-density observation target designation complete.
```

The helicopter vanished beyond the horizon. The vehicle sped in the opposite direction. Between the two points, there was one second.

What that one second meant — no one knew yet.
