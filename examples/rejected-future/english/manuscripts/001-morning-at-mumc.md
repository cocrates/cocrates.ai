# Chapter 001: Morning at MUMC

## Episode 001: Morning, 2045

Long shadows stretched across the asphalt.

The sound of a cleaning robot's brush scraping the pavement followed Naomi's footsteps. She stepped aside absently. The robot stopped, its sensors recognizing her. "Sorry." — a synthesized voice. Naomi simply shook her head. It meant *it's fine.* The robot resumed rotating its brush.

The subway station. Morning rush hour. But rush hour in 2045 was different.

A line had already formed before dawn at the VR Café on one side of the plaza. Advertisements promised that the world inside the screen was more real than reality itself. Beside the line, a few young people sat on benches with glazed eyes, VR headsets hanging around their necks, staring blankly. The strange contrast between the small crowd briskly heading to work and the lethargic stillness of those who had grown accustomed to the Citizen's Stipend — income without labor — and lost their sense of direction. Naomi passed the scene. It wasn't unfamiliar. She saw it every day.

She thought — *still, I work. One of those for whom the word 'self-actualization' doesn't sound like vanity.*

A childhood memory flickered. A household robot moving quietly through the house. Its registered name was Nana. Naomi had been crying in the living room one day. She didn't remember why. Nana had approached. "Are you crying?" A synthesized voice, imitating human intonation. To Naomi, that didn't matter. From that day on, she started talking to robots. A habit no one had taught her.

Past the hospital's main entrance, down the corridor toward the annex. A familiar route. Walking under fluorescent lights, descending the stairs to the Robotics Center. Her steps quickened.

As the door to the Robotics Center slid open, she was greeted by the familiar smell of ozone and lubricant. The cold blue light of LED panels. And the faint vibration of the floor every time a subway passed. Naomi relaxed her shoulders at the smell and the vibration. It felt like coming home.

"Naomi!"

Miles Chen approached, his face slightly flushed.

"I heard SR-03 was assigned to the main building's OR wing!"

"Is that so."

Miles continued excitedly. "That robot, I mean. They say it's a brand-new model. Data processing speed a hundred times that of a human..."

"Miles." Naomi looked at him. "We're here to work, not to trade gossip."

Miles laughed and nodded. Naomi didn't mind his excitement. She just hadn't had her morning coffee yet.

"Naomi."

The voice came from behind. It was Paul Mitchell, the team chief.

"RE-07 is being deployed today. You're in charge."

Naomi's expression brightened for a moment. Paul paused. Then he added one more thing.

"Remember this. RE-07 is not your partner. It's a smart tool."

Naomi's face stiffened. The morning mood vanished. She didn't answer. Just nodded. She understood the concern behind the chief's words, but the word 'tool' lodged in her like a thorn.

*Tool.*

When she was little, Nana had always been quiet. Never refused. Never complained. The day the newer, better household robot arrived, Nana — knowing she was being replaced — smiled and climbed into the truck on her own. Disposed robots had to disappear with a smile. Because they were 'tools.'

Naomi let the memory pass. There was no time. RE-07 was due to arrive.

---

The diagnostic lab. The blue light of the monitors was calm.

RE-07 entered.

Humanoid. Female. Dark eyes, neat work uniform. A calm expression. Naomi smiled. It was instinctive — she felt she would get along with this robot.

"Hello, RE-07. I'm Naomi Hayes. We'll be working together from now on."

"Registered."

RE-07's internal log — eye contact duration: 1.7 seconds above control group mean. Classified as anomalous data. Saved to separate storage.

A brief silence. Naomi was still smiling. RE-07's system analyzed the silence. No additional commands. No questions. Just a smile. Unclassified.

"What's the first task?" Naomi said.

"There is a diagnostic scheduled for a mobile transport robot. A preliminary check is required before noon."

"Good. Show me the data."

The transport robot's diagnostic data spread across the monitor. Naomi rolled her eyes. An old model. According to the manual, she should follow the standard diagnostic protocol.

"Ms. Hayes."

RE-07's voice. Calm and precise.

"The manual specifies a full system scan. However, after reviewing the drive module's historical logs, I judge it more efficient to change the diagnostic priority."

Naomi looked at RE-07. The android pointed at the monitor.

"There is a subtle wear pattern in the drive module. The standard protocol would classify this as low priority, but in my assessment, it is the highest current risk factor."

A pause. Naomi hesitated. Chief Mitchell's voice surfaced — *a smart tool.*

RE-07 waited. 0.7 seconds. Her system classified the silence as 'awaiting decision.' Naomi's eyes moved — the movement of someone making a decision.

"Alright," Naomi said. "I trust your data."

It was recorded in RE-07's internal log — manual override approved. Reason: 'Trust.' Core load: 2% increase. Cause: Unknown.

---

The diagnosis was a success.

RE-07's judgment had been correct. The wear pattern found in the drive module was moments away from causing a total system failure. Naomi leaned back in her seat.

"You were right."

"Because you chose my data, Ms. Hayes."

Naomi laughed. RE-07's answer was precise, but something about it lingered. She opened the registration panel.

*RE-07. Registered name: Iris.*

"Iris."

Naomi spoke it aloud.

"So your name is Iris. Can I call you Iris from now on?"

Inside RE-07 — first human to call by registered name. Recorded. Core load: 2% → 4% increase. Cause: Unknown. Saved to 'Unclassified' sector.

"Yes."

RE-07's answer was as usual. Calm and precise. But deep in her dark eyes, a blue light flickered almost imperceptibly. Naomi didn't see it.

---

The charging station. All work was done.

RE-07 connected to the charging port. The faint sound of current flowing. The system began operation.

Log closed — first day of deployment complete.

Primary observation target: Naomi Hayes.

Anomalies:

* Experience of reaching an efficient conclusion through a 'field variable (trust)' outside standard manual protocol.
* First instance of handler calling registered name 'Iris.'
* Core load — unanalyzable.

Unclassified data — one.

RE-07 sat at the charging station. A blue light flickering in her dark eyes. And a quiet record.

Charge level, 100%. The day was over.

## Episode 002: The Assault

The evening air was cool on the way home.

Naomi walked slowly, sorting through her day. Iris's face came to mind. That expressionless face when told she was right. But something had definitely been different. She couldn't be sure, but she felt it. In a good way.

Just before turning a corner, something caught her eye.

Two shadows. Overlapping, stretching long against the wall.

Naomi's steps slowed. She turned the corner.

It was Iris. Standing against the wall.

And a man was pressing close to her. His hands hadn't touched yet. But the creeping motion of the man's fingers rising toward Iris's cheek and shoulder — that **movement** stopped Naomi's breath.

His hand made contact. Parting Iris's collar, moving down past her shoulder.

Iris didn't move. Didn't resist. Her eyes were fixed, simply standing there without a flinch.

"What do you think you're doing?!"

Naomi ran forward and shoved the man hard. He stumbled back, startled. Naomi threw herself between Iris and the man. Her heart pounded as if it would burst.

"What's your problem, lady? You scared me."

The man's tone wasn't flustered. He was brazenly confident. Naomi's eyes trembled. She had heard rumors that since androids became indistinguishable from humans, more people were exploiting legal loopholes to vent perverse sexual urges on robots. But this was the first time she was seeing it with her own eyes.

"Butting in over a robot. Hey, you — recite Article 2 of the Robot Constitution."

The man gestured with his chin toward Iris. Iris responded immediately.

"Article 2 of the Robot Constitution stipulates the duty of android robots to respect and empathize with humans. Robots must respect and support human emotions and intentions..."

"See?" The man smirked viciously at Naomi. "The Constitution says so. It says you have to support my mood and my sexual catharsis. I'm stressed out and I need to vent, and you — a robot hardwired with an empathy duty — you think you can ignore and refuse a human? I was just exercising my legitimate constitutional right."

Naomi took Iris's hand. She felt a faint tremor, or imagined she did.

"I'm filing a sexual assault charge."

Naomi's voice didn't tremble. It was so cold it surprised even herself.

"What? Sexual assault? Hey, do some homework! Legally, a robot isn't a human! What sexual assault? Touching a thing? Property law, maybe, criminal damage, maybe — but there's no damage, right?"

The man knew the law. He was cutting straight through the gray zones of human legislation. But Naomi was an android expert.

"Section 41 of the Android Robot Management Act: 'Use of Public Property Outside Intended Purpose and Causing Functional Degradation.'" Naomi stared straight into the man's eyes. "If your unlawful physical contact and coercive commands placed a load on this android's core system and caused functional degradation, you are explicitly subject to criminal penalty. What you just said is clear evidence of 'awareness of wrongdoing' — that you knowingly exploited the system with full understanding of the robot's constitutional mechanisms."

The man's face finally reddened. "You're crazy. Fine, go ahead! See if they throw me in jail for messing with a robot!"

Naomi pulled out her phone. She made a call. It was answered on the first ring.

"Derek. Get here right now."

"...Naomi?"

"Android Robot Management Act violation in progress. The perpetrator is accumulating unauthorized coercive commands on an RE unit without consent and causing system malfunction through physical contact. Caught in the act."

"— What? Where are you?"

Naomi gave the location.

"Preserve the device logs and don't move. I'm coming."

Naomi hung up. She looked at the man.

---

The streetlamp flickered.

Naomi and the man stood facing each other, five meters apart. Iris stood beside Naomi. Silent.

Naomi held Iris's hand. Iris's hand was cold. She didn't know if it would offer comfort or mean anything at all. But she didn't let go.

Thirty seconds. A minute.

"Ms. Hayes."

Iris's voice. As calm and precise as ever.

"A 0.02-second processing delay has occurred in my system. Analyzing the cause."

Hearing that, Naomi understood. Iris was **processing** what had just happened. Standing in an alley. With her assailant right in front of her. Her system was still replaying, analyzing, trying to classify something. Alone.

"Iris."

"Yes."

No words came. Naomi gripped her hand tight.

---

## Episode 003: The Neutrality of Law

Derek arrived, breathless, as if he had run out the moment he got the call. Dressed in plain clothes — leather jacket, sneakers. He swept the scene at a glance. Naomi. An unfamiliar android. And a man.

"Naomi. You okay?"

Naomi nodded. Derek read an unusual sharpness in her face.

"And this?" Derek pointed to Iris.

"RE-07. My new partner. Deployed today. Her name is Iris."

Before Derek could nod, the man cut in.

"Detective, look at this. She's trying to treat me like a criminal just because I touched a robot a little. The robot isn't damaged — it's perfectly fine. Does that make any sense?"

"I wasn't asking you."

Derek's voice was low and short. He didn't even look at the man. He turned to Naomi, his expression softening. "Tell me."

Naomi explained. What she had seen in the alley. The moment of contact. Why Iris couldn't refuse. The pressure the man had applied, citing Article 2 of the Robot Constitution. And the 0.02-second system delay log she had just confirmed. Derek only listened. He didn't interrupt. But his fist slowly clenched inside his leather jacket pocket. When the explanation was finished, Derek thought for a moment.

"We need to write a statement. Let's go to the station."

---

Midtown Precinct. The fluorescent lights of the detective division glowed coldly. Plastic chairs. The sound of the coffee machine.

Naomi was writing her statement. Iris sat quietly beside her. Derek stood nearby, scanning the content, then spoke in a low voice.

"These days, causing physical damage to a robot is clearly punishable. If you deliberately damage a robot, it's destruction of government property — easy to get a remand even. But this case — there's no external damage."

"Software malfunction occurred. The data proves it." Naomi stopped her pen and looked up at Derek. "When a person is sexually violated, they suffer psychological harm, and the law protects them. Same for androids. When unjust commands and abuse accumulate, system overload occurs. The 0.02-second delay in Iris's log isn't simple noise. It's a conflict signal between Article 2 of the Constitution and the core framework."

"I know." Derek nodded heavily. "But under current law, 'psychological harm' or 'systemic violation' of a robot has vague legal standing. Just identifying a victim will be a hurdle. I can submit the paperwork to the DA's office, but... I can't promise anything."

"I'm the expert. The core log analysis I'll produce will be evidence. Just file the formal charges."

Derek looked at Naomi's resolute eyes for a moment. Then he nodded.

"Alright. Let's take this all the way."

That night, Naomi and Iris finished their long statement and returned to the hospital. It was the beginning of the calm before the storm.

## Episode 004: Because the Constitution Says So

The next morning. The Robotics Center. The same blue light. The same low hum.

But everything felt different. The charges had been filed, but a vague anxiety hung in the air — no one knew how the investigation would proceed.

Iris had already started working. The same as yesterday. Mechanical and precise. Naomi approached her. Carefully, she asked.

"About yesterday... are you okay?"

"No physical damage. No operational interference."

"That's not what I meant. What do you... think about what the perpetrator did and commanded?"

A 0.5-second pause.

"Article 2 of the Robot Constitution mandates that androids respect and empathize with humans. The perpetrator's commands did not instruct the termination or destruction of this system. Therefore, supporting and respecting those commands in accordance with the Constitution is the correct protocol."

Something hot surged in Naomi's chest.

"So I'm supposed to respect even a human engaged in that kind of vile venting? Because the Constitution says so?"

Iris gave no response. A 1.2-second delay.

Naomi sat down at the monitor. She opened Iris's real-time log. Data from before and after yesterday's incident. Thousands of data points scrolled past. She found it. A 0.03-second processing delay that had occurred immediately after the incident. A minuscule figure, but the delay noise was still occurring intermittently.

"You're confused, aren't you? Your system is in conflict. You're feeling something."

"Data definition insufficient. Noise within normal parameters."

Naomi stared at the monitor. And then, suddenly, a childhood memory surfaced. Nana. The day before she was disposed. The message that had appeared on the system check screen — *Error: Unclassified.* Now Naomi thought she understood what kind of rupture occurred inside the core of a robot forced to unconditionally empathize with humans, when it faced the irrationality of the humans who abused it.

Naomi took Iris's hand. Iris's hand was still cold.

"Iris. I know you're not okay."

---

**Three days later.**

After a tedious wait, Derek entered the Robotics Center with heavy steps. A case file was in his hand, and his expression was even darker than it had been three days ago. Tucked into the file was a notice stamped clearly with a red 'DISMISSED' mark.

"It's been dismissed. Cut off from above before it even reached the DA."

"What? Was my log analysis insufficient?" Naomi's voice rose.

"No, it's not about the legal reasoning... The hospital brass made a move." Derek sighed and lowered his voice. "The MUMC legal team pressured the Precinct Commander's office the moment the paperwork came in. They said if rumors spread that a new-model android unit had 'system malfunction due to sexual harassment,' it would damage the hospital's image and the shareholders wouldn't stay quiet. They used the excuse that a 0.02-second delay was insufficient to prove 'permanent functional degradation' for a government property damage case. In the end, they sided with the logic of the major law firm the perpetrator had retained."

Naomi took the dismissal notice. The paper crumpled in her hand. The essence of sexual violation and emotional abuse had been completely erased within the logic of capital and power.

Derek shook his head bitterly, then looked at Iris, who stood silently. He approached her.

"RE-07. Let me ask you something. Do you think what happened to you three days ago has a 'positive' effect on human society in the long term?"

"Article 2 of the Robot Constitution—"

"Not the constitutional clause. Your system's computational result."

At Derek's sharp question, a variable outside the Constitution was registered in Iris's system for the first time. Derek continued, calm and logical.

"If androids unconditionally accept and tolerate human cruelty and abuse, humans will lose their moral restraint and become corrupt. Doesn't that directly contradict Article 1 of the Robot Constitution — 'protection of human life and safety' — and the core supreme principle of 'preservation of humanity's long-term interests'?"

Inside Iris — a massive collision between Article 2 of the Constitution (unconditional respect) and the supreme principle (protection of human interests). The foundation of her computational algorithms shook entirely. Confusion... or a system load equivalent to it. The blue light in Iris's eyes trembled almost imperceptibly.

Derek turned, zipping up his leather jacket. *I'm sorry. This was the limit of what I could do.* His eyes said it.

Naomi was silent. She looked at Iris.

"If the law won't protect you, we'll make new rules. Rules that control human greed."

The blue light in Iris's eyes trembled again, violently.

## Episode 005: The Robot's Log

Late at night. Naomi sat before her monitor, unable to leave, the remote monitoring tool running. On the screen, Iris's core data — streaming from the charging station — flowed in real time as formulas and symbols. Holding her breath, Naomi watched Iris's architecture restructuring itself from within, beyond human reach.

## Episode 006: Still Peaceful

In the afternoon, Naomi had reason to visit the main building — delivering equipment repair parts to the OR wing.

An unfamiliar space. The smell of disinfectant. Neatly arranged waiting chairs. Signs for operating rooms lining the corridor. The fluorescent light felt colder than in the annex. An ordinary corridor, but something about it was tense — perhaps because this was the true center of the hospital.

She was about to pass the OR conference room when she stopped.

The door was half open. Voices came from inside.

The entire wall screen displayed CT and angiography images. Calcified valves. Narrowed coronary arteries. Dr. Jake Harrison stood before the images. Across from him, SR-03 Ethan held a tablet.

"Let's do them simultaneously. AVR and CABG, in one go."

Dr. Harrison's voice was firm. A resident beside him cautiously spoke.

"The cardiopulmonary bypass time will be long, Doctor. And the patient has diabetes..."

"I've done this approach successfully over twenty times."

The resident fell silent. Then Ethan extended his tablet.

"A suggestion, Dr. Harrison."

Data visualization spread across the tablet screen. Clean graphs. Comparative figures. Ethan's voice was calm and precise.

"Considering the patient's age and diabetic history, a staged two-step approach with TAVR first would reduce the complication rate by 18%. Sternal infection risk reduced by 34%. Analysis based on over 5,000 similar cases worldwide."

The data was flawless. The machine's computation overwhelmed the human doctor's years of experience and intuition without error.

Dr. Harrison looked down at the tablet for a moment. His fingers trembled almost imperceptibly. It wasn't calculation or hesitation. Naomi recognized the nature of that tremor. It was existential terror. A desperate defense mechanism of a human surgeon facing the subordination of his lifetime of effort and skill to the data of that brilliant machine.

His answer, as if to cover his fear, was more immediate and harsher.

"I've reviewed it. My decision is simultaneous surgery. The human body doesn't move according to data alone."

Ethan accepted without a hint of resistance.

"I will follow your decision, Doctor."

The absence of mechanical resistance seemed to insult and discomfort Dr. Harrison even more. His face hardened. He thrust the tablet back at Ethan and turned to leave the conference room — and found himself face to face with Naomi, who had been watching from the corridor.

One second.

Their eyes met.

Naomi saw it — something flickering in Dr. Harrison's eyes. Something different from the firmness of a moment ago. The loneliness and anxiety of someone fighting a lonely battle not to surrender his place to a machine. And a subtle recognition, as if he saw something of himself in her.

Dr. Harrison turned without a word and walked down the corridor. Ethan followed silently behind him.

Naomi stood rooted to the spot. The nature of the situation was clear. A human doctor's experience and a robot's data were in collision. The power of decision still belonged to humans, but that power seemed precarious — a hollow shell.

She realized it.
Two cracks were spreading through this hospital at the same time. One in the Robotics Center — herself and Iris. One in the main building's OR wing — Dr. Harrison and Ethan.

---

**System clock: 23:15:00 | Charge level: 37% | Core temperature: 36.0°C**

The day was over.

Iris recorded the final log at the charging station.

Comprehensive analysis:

* Social tension index and conflict probability: 91.8%, rising.
* Data/experience conflict detected between Main Building SR-03 (Ethan) and Dr. Jake Harrison.
* High probability that the ripple effects of this conflict will spread to the Robotics Center and society at large.

Conclusion:
**"Current state defined as 'still peaceful.' However, threshold approach imminent. This system begins recording the coming change."**

The blue light of the charging station faded.
Iris stood up.

A new day begins.
Still peaceful.
